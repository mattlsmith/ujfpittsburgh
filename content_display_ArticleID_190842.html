"The Partnership Report"- An On The Scene Bulletin From Karmiel & Misgav

August 2, 2006


To our dear friends and partners,

Today is Wednesday, the 22nd day of the war. After 48 hours of "relative quiet", we began our morning with a rash of sirens (20 incoming alerts were sounded until the afternoon hours) and orders to enter into the shelters and "secure rooms". The rocket launchers of the Hezbollah began once more to bomb the north.

By the afternoon, over 160 Katyusha rockets were fired, of them 14 on Karmiel. Fortunately, all 14 landed in open areas surrounding the city.

As we had feared, the quiet of the past two days did not herald the end of the war and although much is being said and written about a ceasefire, the end has yet to come into sight?

The army and other security agencies estimated that with the end of the "48 hours" there would be a harsh response by the Hezbollah. This estimate was passed on to the relevant municipalities and officials who subsequently instructed residents as to appropriate actions.

Once again, the streets of Karmiel emptied and the pathways of Misgav communities were practically deserted?

Aside from a few extra-ordinary exceptions, businesses are closed and other than "critical employees", the region's labor force has been instructed to remain at home and/or within secure zones.

The organized activities for children and young people continue in the region's shelters and secure rooms, as well as performances by known Israeli performers and actors and visits by numerous VIP's.

In an effort to raise the city's morale, the city administration prepared "Karmiel is Strong" t-shirts to be worn by city employees and volunteers as they distribute food and other supplies to the city's shelters.

Today the city was visited by a senior official of IDB Holding Company Ltd and the CEO of "Supersal" Supermarkets, an Israeli supermarket chain that since the beginning of the war has contributed significant amounts of non-perishable food and hygienic products to residents throughout the Galilee sitting in the shelters.


The bomb in the picture was launched by the Israeli Air Force on a Hezbollah position in Lebanon with a special dedication in memory of Eshael Damti,
In Memory of Eshael
originally from Karmiel, who was killed by the Hezbollah during a Katyusha rocket attack on the Haifa Railroad Yards where he worked. The dedication was written by his friends from the IAF base where he served during his years of regular army service.

The difficulties and fears of the past three weeks has brought about a "temporary evacuation" of many residents from their homes and a search for safer quarters until quiet is restored. Nurit Mazor, who in normal times manages Karmiel's public library, today operates a data bank that brings together city residents looking for a temporary respite from the warfare and those in the center and southern regions of the country willing and able to host them. Until today, the "hospitality desk" has handled approximately 4,000 requests.
What are the options offered city residents?

• Temporary quarters in a "Tent City" established at the "Nitzanim" Beach along the Mediterranean- until today some 900 individuals have been referred to this framework.

• Children's camps sponsored by Jewish Agency and national youth movements- until today some 500 young people have participated in this program.

• Home hospitality with families in the central and southern regions- until today some 700 families have been placed in these alternative "homes".

• Beach cabins sponsored by the City of Herzliya- until today some 100 individuals have been referred to this framework.

• A weeklong "get-away" for single-parent families sponsored by the Sheraton chain of hotels at the company's hotels in Tel Aviv, Ramat Gan and at the Dead Sea- until today some 180 individuals have been referred to this framework.

• Round trip transportation for daylong "get-aways" for those who wish just to get some fresh air out of rocket range

Nurit noted that the Israeli public has shown a wonderful volunteer spirit and tremendous goodwill. She has received offers from more than 1,000 families willing to host northern residents including round-trip transportation! The efforts coordinated by Nurit and her staff do not include referrals arranged directly between contacts made through the media.

In Misgav, as I was told by Liat Belcar, director of the region's community center, the question of "temporary evacuation" was handled in a different manner, concentrating on children and teenagers.

The community center, in cooperation with its parent organization, the Israel Community Centers Ltd, made contact with three community centers (Ma'ale Adumim, Cochav Yair and Gan Yavneh) and together with them, a hospitality program and various activities were developed for the young people of Misgav.

• 350 children from grades 4-6 enjoyed a day of fun at the Shefayim Water Park

• 500 children from grades 7-8 were hosted at three of the community centers mentioned above

• Regional sports teams held training camps outside the area

• Kindergarten and pre-school children enjoyed special activities within the communities 
 
In spite of the war and the difficulties in coping with the situation, there are rays of normalcy, such the impressive achievements of the region's "Hapoel Misgav Swim Team" at the Israeli National Championships for ages 10-14.

"In spite of the war and Katyusha rockets, the Hapoel Misgav Swim Team achieved some impressive achievements that the recent national championships, reported Yaacov Landman (Yuvalim), swimming coordinator for the Misgav Community Center.

The most outstanding achievement was that of Tal Kidron from the Misgav community of Tsurit who during the championships was awarded the "outstanding swimmer" award for swimmers ages 10-12 and three personal gold medals, a silver medal and a bronze in the team relay. 

The members of the Hapoel Misgav Swim Team were forced to cope with a training program harshly affected by the current hostilities. As the war began, they were forced to practice in unfamiliar pools, run for shelter during a "training camp" held in the north and continued practicing away from family and friends in pools in the Jerusalem area. Here is where we express our appreciation to the Jerusalem Swimming Association for their warm hospitality and assistance.

The team also achieved additional awards in the 10-14 age level:
The Misgav team placed 8th out of the 38 teams that participated in the championships

Meital Engelhart a 12-year-old swimmer from Avtalion was twice declared national champion with two gold medals

Drobot Dennis, age 14 from Karmiel finished 1st runner-up in four events

Shalev Zimran from Shecknia won two silver medals

Next week the senior swim team is scheduled to participate in the Israeli Nationals at Wingate."
Good Luck to all our swimmers and coaches!  

In closing, I want to bring to you a report issued by the Jewish Agency describing its response to the war and the tremendous effort made by the agency in cooperation with UJC Federations to provide safe quarters, solace and supplies for the people of the north.

Israel Crisis: Update on Jewish Agency Response
Update from August 1st


Children from the North enjoying a Jewish Agency Summer Camp

Summer Camps, Week Three:
More than 9,600 currently participating, including around 500 new immigrants
While the program of activities for each summer camp varies, they all include an array of challenging outdoor activities – sports, swimming, hiking – creative indoor activities – arts and crafts, drama workshops, and gymnastics – and as many field trips as possible to places like the Keftsuba Amusement Park, Shefayim Water Park, museums and more. Morning workshops address educational and personal issues, such as coping with stress and anxiety, and night activities include sing-alongs, magic shows, performances and campfires. For new immigrant children, activities also include Hebrew ulpan courses in order to continue their integration into Israeli culture.

 
Waiting in the bomb shelter of the Jewish Agency Absorption Center in Nahariya

Provided for Under Fire: Emergency Amenities for Bomb Shelters
More than 700 air conditioners, 625 televisions and 2,650 emergency lighting to an initial network of 1,179 bomb shelters across the North
The current crisis exposed the neglected state of more than 5,000 bomb shelters throughout the North – each of which is filled to capacity with local residents and families, huddled together, hoping for safety. For both new immigrants in Jewish Agency bomb shelters and residents in Northern-area bomb shelters, the waiting period in between sirens seems endless. The atmosphere in the shelters is tense and the physical discomfort is unbearable. To improve the quality of living in Northern bomb shelters and ensure its up-to-date safety, the Jewish Agency, with support from Jewish communities worldwide, is supplying air conditioning units, emergency lighting fixtures, water fountains, televisions and cable connections. Additional provisions are being made for Jewish Agency Absorption Center bomb shelters, including supplementary staff to address psychological needs and provide round-the-clock care, emergency equipment, food and water, portable chemical toilets and hygienic products. Thousands more bomb shelters throughout the North await your support to facilitate their improvement. 
 
A Family Mission from Florida visiting children at a Jewish Agency Summer Camp

One People: The Global Jewish Community Comes to Israel
14 solidarity missions visited Jewish Agency activities from the US, Canada, Europe and the Netherlands
Driven by their support for Israel, these individual, family and group missions are dedicating their time to visiting Jewish Agency Absorption Centers and Summer Camps where they interact with Israeli children and adults and hear first-hand reactions to the current crisis. These meetings were incredibly powerful for both visitors and Israelis, as the expression of support and care from abroad strengthens everyone's ability to persevere through the crisis. Upcoming missions from Argentina, Brazil, Canada and the US are currently being coordinated with the field.
 

Story from the Field: Dedicated Staff Remains in the Line of Fire
 
Jewish Agency Staff Helping Residents of the Tapuz Absorption Center in Nahariya
As relentless terror rains across the skies of northern Israel, one million of the area’s two million residents have fled to the safety of the center of the country. But as shops and businesses in Nahariya closed, the staff of the Jewish Agency's Tapuz absorption center were not about to leave behind the new immigrants from Ethiopia who still call the absorption center home.

"We need to be here around the clock," says Tapuz's director Chava Maimon. Although the Jewish Agency has been able to relocate many of the 250 residents, including children now in Jewish Agency safe summer camps, many of the most vulnerable are living in the shelter. "Those having to remain—the elderly, families with young children or those who had nowhere else to go—have barely had time to adjust to the tremendous changes involved in coming from Ethiopia to Israel. Now they are caught in a war and we must make sure that they are cared for." 

Haimanot Malada, a “housemother” at the center, arrived from Ethiopia in 1991 during the first Gulf War. “I remember having to place my baby daughter inside a special plastic incubator so she would be safe in case of a chemical attack,” recalling her own traumatic experience. “That is why I’m determined to be here now to help these young mothers.”

From Haifa to Metulla, Jewish Agency workers in the north have left their own homes to stay with the new immigrants. Many of these dedicated workers have families who are also living in bomb shelters. Even more remarkable is the Jewish Agency staff from other parts of Israel that are volunteering to come up to the north to help. “These colleagues are literally placing themselves at risk to help out,” explains Maimon. "The spirit of togetherness is incredible."

The Jewish Agency is underway with a major effort to keep pace with meeting the 24/7 basic needs of those still living in bomb shelters in the 13 absorption centers throughout the North. In addition to psychological assistance and activities for the children, provisions include food and water, portable chemical toilets and emergency equipment. “Just imagine what it is like to not even be able to go to the supermarket to buy the simple basics. They are relying on us for everything,” says Maimon.

Donations to support Jewish Agency emergency programs can be made through UJC, local federations, Keren Hayesod and the Spirit of Israel. For more information, please visit our website: www.jewishagency.org

That's all for today, let us pray that quiet will be restored and peace and security will return to the region and the country.


Sincerely yours,

Yael Raich
