Operation Promise: A Summary

Our Jewish story begins with a promise, passed from generation to generation like a golden chain, etched in our collective memories and recorded in our books. This promise represents our hope to be a free people, and to create a better future; it represents our dream to be a compassionate people, whose purpose it is to make the world a better place.

The United Jewish Federation of Pittsburgh has joined federations throughout North America in launching Operation Promise, a special campaign to help provide expanded lifesaving services to Ethiopian Jews and Jews in the former Soviet Union (FSU).

Operation Promise has a Pittsburgh goal of $2.3 million as part of a North American goal of $160 million. The funding will be provided over a three-year period and will supplement the UJF Annual Community Campaign. Edgar and Sandy Snyder are co-chairs of Operation Promise.

"Operation Promise is an important commitment we must make to fulfill our obligation to take care of those needy Jews who rely on us so they can live with some modicum of dignity," said Barbara Burstin, UJF Chair of the Board. "Our leadership recognizes that it's not only important to increase their commitment to the 2006 UJF Annual Campaign but to also support Operation Promise for the life-saving services it will provide to these vulnerable populations."

"In deciding whether to take the Operation Promise chairmanship," Edgar Snyder said, "we knew that if Karen Shapira were here today and knowing the great work she had accomplished on behalf of Ethiopian Jews and Jews in the Former Soviet Union, she would have asked us to do this."

Karen, a past chair of the UJF and a national Jewish community leader, went on a fact-finding mission to Ethiopia in 2003 on behalf of United Jewish Communities to assess the situation there. She had also traveled throughout the former Soviet Union during the 1990s on behalf of the Pittsburgh and the North American Jewish communities to offer assistance and leadership regarding the rebirth of organized Jewish communities in the FSU.
