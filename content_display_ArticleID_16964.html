The Blue and Yellow Frontier: A Jewish Home in the Making

By Sharon Schatz Rosenthal


This is my plan: the yellow and white plates will be for dairy foods and the dark blue ones will be for meat. My rationale? Yellow reminds me of eggs, which reminds me of toast, which reminds me of cereal, which reminds me of milk. Since blue is a darker color, it's a good match for meat, which is also kind of dark (well, steak is, anyway). See, this whole separation of food and plate is new to me. I grew up Reform and tend to associate the word "kosher" with Passover--not my everyday life. But as my wedding date gets closer and closer, I'm realizing that "my" everyday life is becoming "our" everyday life.

Glenn told me early on in our relationship that he wanted to keep a kosher kitchen when he got married. I didn't think much of it, then. I guess it didn't really hit home until we were standing in Crate & Barrel with our registry kit and Glenn asked me which plates I'd like for our dairy meals. Suddenly, my future was right there in front of me on colorful, mass-produced ceramic. What was I getting myself into? Could, I, Sharon Schatz, the girl who doesn't even know how to make a grilled cheese sandwich or cook a chicken, spend my life using different sets of plates, or more importantly, a different set of religious rules?

Glenn grew up Conservative. Not only does his family keep a kosher kitchen, but Glenn is one of those people who knows the words to ALL the songs and prayers when we go to services for the high holidays. In fact, he likes to go to synagogue for the whole day on Yom Kippur. When I was a kid, my family went in the morning, stayed until about 1 P.M., then went home. You could find me in the closet stuffing my face with Doritos before we broke the fast. Well, that's how it was for a while, anyway.

My parents were Reform until I was a teenager and my two younger brothers were born. They decided that they wanted the boys to be raised Conservative and there was sort of a religious renaissance in my house. My mom, who didn't have much of a Jewish education growing up, began going to synagogue on Saturday morning and was considering becoming a Bat Mitzvah. Friday night was suddenly "Shabbat night" and I remember the mad rush to find enough yarmulkes for the lighting of the candles. Unfortunately, this was not a happy time for me. I resented my parents' insistence that we follow these new rules. Sadly, the residue of these feelings has stayed with me.

Glenn and I always joke that we have to get over our "religious differences." But, it's not really a joke. I'd always planned on marrying a Jewish man and raising my children Jewish. But kosher kitchens, marathon high holiday services, and the "no eating out during Passover" custom are somewhat new to me and a reminder of my parents' rules. I think my biggest obstacle is my resentment, which, I know, has absolutely nothing to do with Glenn.

But somehow, that afternoon at Crate & Barrel gave me a sort of clarity. As I stood looking at our soon-to-be kosher plates for our soon-to-be-kosher kitchen, I took a look at my soon-to-be husband. Glenn is amazing. He's funny, brilliant, sensitive, and adorable. AND he went to overnight camp on the east coast, like me--what more could I ask for? The truth is--unlike the situation with my parents (whom I know, did not have bad intentions), he's not imposing strict rules on me. He tells me his visions of our future household and how we're going to raise our future children, but I know that the other half of that equation is up to me.

We're building a new home together and we can create our own traditions. Maybe we'll follow the rituals that are important to Glenn, as well as some of the ones I actually liked from my own childhood. Like my family's dorky tradition of parading around the kitchen table singing songs after lighting the Hanukkah candles. Maybe my feelings about religion don't have to be about the past. And if I think hard enough, maybe not all of those memories are negative.

Last night was the first night of Hanukkah. After we lit the candles, I led Glenn around the kitchen table and belted out my family's silly Hanukkah song that no one else in this world seems to know. We made latkes (potato pancakes) using our new food processor, while the chicken dinner was cooking.

By the end of it, I was the one holding the spatula and frying our potato concoction. Afterwards, we sat down to eat, using our meat plates. Actually, they're our temporary meat plates. The new ones that we registered for aren't here, yet. But, they'll come.

As I sit at the computer in our room, I notice Glenn's Harry Potter book, his yellow laundry bag left over from camp, and the toys he bought for my cats. I smile as I think about my future with him-- our future. And I look forward to the coming of the blue plates.
