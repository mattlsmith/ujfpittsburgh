"The Partnership Report"- An On The Scene Bulletin From Karmiel & Misgav

August 7, 2006


To our dear friends and partners,

Today is Monday, the 27th day of the war. Thankfully, we had a "quiet" morning. In fact, until the afternoon hours, while I sit here to write my daily report, we've had no reports of Katyusha rockets falling in the region. We "made due" with the five siren alarms that forced us to look for cover in our shelters and secure rooms.

However, this has all changed just as I sat to write to you for at this hour we've received a barrage of six rockets, fortunately, all of them in open areas and causing no damage.

"Relative quiet" in the region is an "important commodity" that allows both residents and public officials to take stock of the situation, organize for the coming hours and days and plan for "the day after" the war.

Adi Eldar, the mayor of Karmiel "exploited" the morning hours to tour the city's shelters and meet with local residents. During his tour Adi met up with Israeli journalist, author and television personality, Avirima Golan who was volunteering her time to read to children holed up in the city's shelters.

Adi was quite impressed by the variety of activities offered children in the shelters such as "War through the Eyes of Children", a program that allows kids to express their feelings and thoughts through drawing.

The drawing to the left was drawn by a young boy from Karmiel who expressed the threat and fear he felt from the Katyusha rockets. The writing in the drawing is the Russian expression for "ouch", an expression of pain.

Assistance from the Home Front Command
Adi also requested and received a contingent of Home Front Command soldiers as reinforcements for the city's hard-pressed employees. The soldiers will assist the municipality to distribute 2,500 hot meals to the city's elderly in their homes and shelters, load equipment at the city's logistical center based at the Social Services Administration, and distribute it throughout the city's 600 shelters.

Last week the City of Karmiel was authorized by the Home Front Command to return to work on the condition that "secure rooms" were available for all municipality employees and visitors.

In order to allow employees to come to work "free of worries about the kids", the city organized a day camp for employees' children in a shelter at one of the city's schools.

Adi Eldar (center) visits a city sponsored day camp set up in a bomb shelter for employees' children
Erez Kriesler, the mayor of the Misgav Regional Council and his staff are hard at work improving physical security conditions throughout the council's communities and facilities. This week, for example the council's engineering and maintenance departments will complete a major job of strengthening shelters and secure zones in kindergartens and pre-school daycare centers in the region's northern communities of Camun, Michmanim, Tel Al, Lavon, Tsurit, Gilon, Shoreshim and Harashim. The improvements are being made in 15 kindergartens and daycare centers that have been open for activities throughout the war.

The improvements include:
• special window coatings to prevent glass shattering and splinters that might occur because of an explosive blast
• specific instructions for community representatives on how to neutralize dangerous objects and fire dangers that might result from hostile activities
• first aid, rescue and fire extinguishing equipment was distributed to the communities
 
The 56,000NIS ($12,727) cost of these improvements was funded from the regional council's Emergency Preparedness Budget. 

Erez Kiesler, Mayor of Misgav donating blood
Between the various shelter tours, meetings with residents and managing the response to the crisis, the council sponsored a blood donation drive, organized by the Human Resources Department in which 72 donations of blood were collected. The mayor Erez Kriesler and his deputy Baruch Rosen were among the many employees and residents who made the effort to contribute blood. According to Dina Neckler, the campaign's organizer, the council will continue to sponsor these drives in light of the excellent response by residents and a new donation drive will be organized shortly.

Hanna Dinman, a resident of Koranit who donated her blood told us, "until now I had been afraid to donate blood, my first time was six months ago. Today I'm back again, because of the war, I felt a need to do this. Yesterday I volunteered to operate a playcenter for the children in Koranit and today I'm here to donate blood. We all do what we can?"

The "State of Emergency" continues and with it, numerous activities for our children in both the region and outside rocket range in order to allow them to relax a bit and enjoy what remains of their summer vacation.

Headlined "Singing & Dancing with the Stars", the Misgav Community Center Dance School & Studio initiated and organized an outing on August 3, 2006 for some 100 dancers from grades 7-12 who are active members of area dance troupes. The young people traveled to the center of the country where they were members of the audience for a recording of the popular Israeli TV program "Kochav Nolad" ("A Star is Born" Israel's version of American Idol) and returned home late in the night, happy and excited from the fun time they had.

Gili Gable and her young Misgav dancers
Michal Cohen, the school's director told us about Gili Gable, a newly released soldier and Dance School graduate, accompanied by members of the school's faculty who has begun to teach line dancing to young children ages 6-9 years old, both boys and girls from the Misgav communities of Shoreshim, Yuvalim and Atzmon. The children it was reported cooperated, danced and enjoyed every moment of this wonderful activity of dance and movement within the framework of the activities organized for kids throughout the region.

As you can well imagine, the 27-day long state of emergency has had a tremendous impact on the daily functioning of those residents forced to depart from their daily routine, reside in shelters for an extended period and cope with a wide range of emotions such as fear, hopelessness and economic uncertainty.

Shula Menachem, director of Karmiel's Social Services Administration reported that aside from the fact that residents are unable to conduct a normal daily routine to meet their most basic needs, they are also forced to cope with a variety of sociological questions related to their being "forced" to exist with others while sitting in the shelters. Serious social conflicts such as personal or "ethnic" clashes (e.g. between various immigrant groups) broke out in a number of public shelters and required the active intervention of the city's social services department.

A team of city social workers underwent a crash course in conflict resolution and mediation to be able to intervene and resolve the varied and unpredictable conflicts liable to breakout in these "flash point" shelters.

Shula also noted that it is important for the "residents of the shelters" to have a framework where the can be heard, "let off steam", have a normal conversation, get encouragement and see the light at the end of the tunnel?

She also reported that an increasing number of individuals are turning to welfare services for help, including families who had never previously been in the department's "radar". These "new applicants" are generally those suffering from severe anxiety attacks. They are people caught up in governmental bureaucracy and without any source of income (the unemployed, newly handicapped individuals who have applied to the National Insurance Institute for compensation, but yet to receive an answer concerning their application, etc). They are young couples forced to stay at home with their children because of a lack of a proper daycare framework, which prevents them from going to work and they are those working families who have lower income because of the war but still need to pay the bills.

Orna Schweitzer, director of Misgav's Social Services Department and Sarit Alkali director of the council's Psychological Services reported that the crisis has brought the two departments to operate jointly, as a single unit.

The combined service includes joint visits and tours of the region's communities, meetings with council residents, families and community leadership. The combined service also works together in distributing aid packages to needy families, referring contributions to the needy, providing support to individuals and groups, including economic assistance to families in distress.

The joint emergency unit is comprised of nine psychologists and seven social workers who reach the council's 35 communities in joint task forces.

The unit has also begun to formulate an intervention and aid program for "the day after" the war ends.

In an effort to provide a community response for various sectors of the population in light of the situation, a special parent-children program known as "Tziach V'Tzeva" ("Talk and Draw") has been developed in a collaborative effort between the Misgav Social Services Department and Psychological Services.

The objective of the program is to assist parents and children to cope on an emotional level with the current state of emergency and the difficulties facing the home front. The activity is currently funded by an emergency grant received from the Ministry of Internal Affairs.

The program brings together small groups of parents and their children with specially trained group therapists for a variety of creative activities and conversation concerning their emotions as they are related to the situation. The group will discuss various psychological affects effecting children and adults because of the continuing crisis; legitimize the harsh emotions stirred by this crisis; discuss issues and emotions arising from the children's drawings such as fear and anger and initiate an effort to build and enhance both children's and their parents' abilities to cope.

Within the framework of this new project, Farach Farach from the Misgav Psychological Service and Yair Levine from the Social Services Deportment visited the Misgav Bedouin community of Camana. In residents' homes, the pair met with two groups for a 1½-hour discussion with each group. One group was composed of some 15 participants and the other had approximately 25 parents and children. Each group was composed of a group of mothers and their children ages 3-13.

During one of the meetings, an alert was sounded forcing the staff and participants to move to a secure room. This event allowed for a mutual experience and a joint effort in coping with the threat of this current situation.

Both the staff and the participants reported that the meeting was significant, contributed greatly to their emotional welfare and allowed for mothers to learn how best to cope with their children's distress.

Orna Schweitzer, director of Misgav's Social Services Department said that the department plans to organize additional similar meetings throughout the week and next, particularly, in the Jewish communities of the northern Misgav region.


That's all for today, let us pray that quiet will be restored and peace and security will return to the region and the country.


Sincerely yours,
Yael Raich
