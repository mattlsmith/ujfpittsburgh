A Message from UJF Chair, Barbara Burstin



For Rosh Hashanah services I went to Chicago to be with family and to hear my son-in-law who is a rabbi deliver his sermons. I loved the experience for a number of reasons, but what I want to share with you is some small part of what Elliot talked about. He cited various people who had witnessed pivotal moments in Jewish history and how they had reacted. And how he, himself,  felt in 1995 living in Jerusalem just starting his rabbinical training when he heard the news that Rabin had been assassinated. He knew, as he put it, that the wheels of Jewish history were turning.

Elliot went on to talk about how at these historic moments opportunities must be seized, destiny must be embraced. He quoted a 1956 speech entitled "Fate and Destiny" by Rabbi Joseph Soloveitchik, one of the great leaders of 20th century Jewry. To be a people of fate, Soloveitchik said, is to be a people who resign themselves to a condition, to a set of circumstances, to be passive participants in the unfolding of time. To be a people of destiny signifies a people able to seize an opportunity, a people who have chosen to chart a deliberate and conscious existence. Jews are a people of destiny.

I believe that here and now in 2006 that this is a moment of destiny for the Jewish people, when opportunities must be seized, when destiny must be shaped, a time when the wheels of Jewish history are indeed turning. They are turning in Israel and they are turning in America, as well, but for different reasons.

We all followed events this summer in Israel with great pain and consternation. But to so many observers this is only round one. The future is clouded. As I said when we held a rally for Israel this summer at the field outside Community Day, Israel is facing a threat to its very existence, that from here on in we cannot take its continued existence as a given. The implications of that are enormous, mind boggling, sobering. In Chicago at Elliot’s synagogue every congregant got this card entitled on one side "Embrace Your Destiny" and on the other side a list of what you should do:
 
1. Give to the Israel Emergency Campaign 
2. Join AIPAC (American Israel Public Affairs Committee)  
3. Read the Jerusalem Post or Haaretz for honest reporting and finally 
5. Speak- Be an advocate for Israel- in your community, in your schools and most of all, in your home.……

The wheels of Jewish history are turning in America as well, but the threat is not from those on the outside who hate us, but from those on the inside who are indifferent to us. In America we face dwindling numbers, skyrocketing assimilation. Our annual campaign this year was the highest in our history, a great achievement thanks to so many, but we have to recognize that we actually had fewer donors than ever before due to natural attrition- people moving away or passing away. We need to reach out more in our community, to old timers and newcomers, particularly to the young, and tie them more firmly to our Jewish people, or we will have no future. What good will all our efforts be if we are the last generation to care? 

The fact that 70% of the young adults in the Pittsburgh area are unaffiliated with any Jewish agency or organization - religious or secular - to me is shocking and totally unacceptable and Federation is working diligently to make a big dent in those numbers. That is our mission, our mandate. Ultimately we will be judged by how well we succeed in our efforts at reaching out to the unaffiliated and underaffiliated. Yet at the same time we must continue to respond to those Jews both far and near who need our help, and we must continue to strengthen the Jewish core. It is an awesome responsibility, but one that the Federation continues to take on with great seriousness and commitment, and I am proud to be part of that.

I truly believe that we have what one historian of the Roosevelt era called a "Rendezvous with Destiny". And we must seize the opportunity to shape that destiny, to build the kind of Jewish community we would like to see in the future. We have that responsibility as individuals and as a Federation. Either we go forward, or we will go backward.

These are not easy times. Thomas Paine in December 1776, in the midst of a fight for independence against the British, wrote a famous essay entitled "The Crisis" that began with the famous words: "these are the times that try men’s souls." So too today. We are not waging a  revolution, but we are fighting a war and we, you and me and all of us together, must not be found wanting. There is much we are proud of this year, but there is still much we, as individuals and as a Federation, have left to do. We need to work harder and give harder. We can and we will!  We are not a people of fate; we are a people of destiny.
