"The Partnership Report"- An On The Scene Bulletin From Karmiel & Misgav

July 27, 2006


To our dear friends and partners,
The Aftermath Of Today's Rocket Attack On Karmiel

Today is Thursday, the 16th day of the war. The sirens and sounds of explosions began early in the morning and continue even as I write my daily report in the early evening hours. Just now a siren went off followed by the sound of explosions. I'm in the "secure room" documenting these events in real-time. Earlier a round of rockets caused severe, direct damage to a Karmiel home and the entrance of the city's civic auditorium. Fortunately, one Katyusha hit the ground without exploding. There is property damage, five people were injured and others suffered from shock. Throughout the day numerous rounds fell in the area and I hope that this was it for today.

The continued state of war and the burden placed on public officials requires that additional, fresh forces be enlisted. The Volunteer Units of Karmiel and Misgav that serve their respective communities so well throughout the year have taken on in full vigor additional tasks as part of the war effort and recruited additional volunteers from throughout the region to help when and where they can.

Volunteers Caring for Misgav Area Children
So what are the tasks of the wartime volunteers? They are responsible for distribution of food to the various shelters, the elderly and other handicapped people, go shopping for those who fear or are unable to leave their homes, visit with various homebound individuals and conduct activities for the region's children.  They also help with the administrative needs of the area's various command posts, pack food packages and packages with diapers and other product for infants and transport people throughout the area. Limor Sela, director of the Volunteer Unit of the City of Karmiel said, "in times of trouble, everyone comes together to help one another. It is wonderful to see this spirit of volunteerism throughout the city in a way that warms the heart and raises our morale."

In a gesture of our shared fate, the Misgav Regional Council, at the initiative of Roma Manor, the council's managing director initiated assistance to the City of Karmiel. In cooperation with Misgav's Volunteer Unit volunteers were recruited to volunteer their services in Karmiel and the council placed a number of council vehicles at their disposal to assist them in their work. These volunteers will assist the Karmiel municipality in the distribution of food and hot meals to those in the shelters and other handicapped individuals.

Hanna Kovel, the managing director of the City of Karmiel said, "thanks to the Jewish Agency's Partnership 2000 program we have excellent relations between the Misgav Regional Council and the City of Karmiel and we thank the Misgav council for their welcome initiative." Hanna also noted the support, help and love that the region has received from the City of Pittsburgh and its Jewish Community. Within the first days of the war, representatives of the UJF and the chairpersons of Partnership 2000 were among the first to ask the region's local authorities how they could help. Within a few days, we were informed that the Federation was prepared to assist in the purchase of personal protective gear for the region's volunteers and activists as they tour the area, outside the shelters and "secure rooms" to assist and meet residents' needs.


The continued state of warfare and the large number of injured (soldiers & civilians) in the north, has cause a shortage in fresh blood in the region. A few days ago, the Misgav community of Moreshet organized a blood drive and collected 64 blood donations. Another drive is scheduled for August 2, this time sponsored by the Clalit Health Services, the MDA Bank Blood and the Misgav Regional Council that made a special call to eligible residents requesting that they take the time to make a needed blood donation.

Reuhama Raz, MK and with her the committee chairpersons of Israel's Knesset visited Karmiel today within the framework of VIP visits to learn about the needs of the city and its residents. 

Once again, I've selected an article that I hope will provide you with a sense of the situation through the eyes of the people who are living it. The following is an article published in Ha'aretz in the Tuesday July 25 edition, written by Ron Shapiro, a resident of Mitzpe Aviv (Misgav) about life in the shadow of the "secure room".

"The Secure Room Diary" Ron Shapiro
Yo-Yo Came Home

It was late into the night when the noise of the jets and helicopters that roared across the black sky finally let down, hours after the screams of the sirens and ensuing explosions that evening. It must have been somewhere around 3AM when the pilots' decided that they too needed a short break. My dozing off that was allowed by the welcomed quiet was suddenly disturbed by a familiar sound. It wasn't threatening like a lethal round of rockets or the cry of the siren that usually warns of an approaching rocket attack.

Rather it was the type of cry that was something altogether different, quiet, and pleasant and nearby. It was a cry that had only meaning. Yo-Yo, the darling of our house, the only one of its residents loved by all without any pre-conditions, returned home.

Late Saturday night, with our return from a few days out of rocket range, we were surprised not to be greeted with the usual ritual- a dash towards the car followed by a sluggish roll in the garden. The next day as well we didn't see the end of a gray fuzzy tail or hear a meow.

Now here she was in the "secure room", piled in between the three young girls who had crowded together on two mattresses for two nights in a row. It is difficult to explain the relief one can feel from seeing a scrawny, gray little cat. The fear that a few days of being left alone and of the unknown was such a scorching insult that she left home, proved false. Maybe this was a senseless fear. The lost cat that Pinny had told us about last week had proven his loyalty and returned home.

In any case, as the fear faded, I found the renewed strength to come out of the basement, where we had all slept in an effort to be less exposed to the current dangers, to the ground floor. It was close to 4:30AM, but cats still need to eat something, particularly after a few days of trusting the natural hunting skills. It seems that someone had already filled the cat's bowl to the top.

The night before someone asked if we should still leave the empty bowl in the kitchen for a cat that may never return. In the meantime, we can forget that question and try to get some sleep. Even the "early bird doesn't always have to catch the worm", before sunrise.

A few minutes after spreading out on the sofa, next to Yo-Yo who didn't surrender her space until she received additional signs of affection and the eyes closed, my ears began to hear some dull explosions. Were they from rounds that fell on Haifa or the Bay area? It was heard to know. The sirens didn't work; a quick review of the morning news provided no reports concerning events of this type at the specific hour. It seems that the source of the noise was from the back of a truck that passed the house on the road below, or maybe I imagined or dreamed it. After all of the real booming sounds, the mind begins to manufacture them on their own. As the day began we all tried to create an atmosphere of normalcy. The children's day camps were operating as usual. Each community organized its own activities for the children and teenagers. However, at about 4PM the alarm was sounded and two more followed. After a few minutes in the "secure room", it seemed that once again, for now anyways the Katyusha rockets preferred Akko and the Haifa Bay area. We could return to our normal activities.
Children from Mitzpe Aviv in the community's organized activities

By the end of the week, our community center had renewed its activity, albeit in a reduced format. The number of visitors was limited to no more than 200 people at any one time. This was the limit of the "secure room". While there were no special classes being held, you could still go swimming or use the fitness room. Things were a bit slow there, I was told. Maybe many still preferred to be out of rocket range or maybe the thought of having to run for the "secure room" in a wet bathing suit and sitting there with a group of sweaty strangers did not sound particularly pleasant.

We will continue to make due with our own private "secure room". Now that Yo-Yo's back, it seems all that more secure.

That's all for today, let us pray that quiet will be restored and peace and security will return to the region and the country.

Our next report will be on Monday.

Shabbat Shalom,

Yael Raich
