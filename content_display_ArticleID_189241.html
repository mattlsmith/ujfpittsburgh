Wednesday July 26, 2006 | Nahariya, Israel

"Bring Udi Home"
by Harriet J. Dobin

“I will bring the moon for him, we are two bodies and one soul.”

 

Displaying a remarkable combination of grit and controlled emotion, 30-year-old Karnit Goldwasser of Nahariya stepped to the podium on Day Four of the UJC Solidarity Mission. Calmly and passionately, she told 70 American Jewish visitors that she writes every night to Ehud, or “Udi,” her husband, one of the two soldiers captured in a raid by Hizbollah on July 12th.

 

“I know that he will be back.”

 

For mission members, the surprise visit by Karnit, her mother, and Ehud’s father was the highlight of an emotional final day. The day’s itinerary included a tour of an Israeli Air Force base, and a visit to the thriving coastal city of Ashkelon, where Hamas rockets  are a constant threat. They also heard closing remarks by former Prime Minister Ehud Bara, who thanked the delegates for “crossing half the globe in these testing times to be here with us.

 

“We are all united in our right to have a Jewish, flourishing, democratic, Zionist state,” said Prime Minister Barak.

 

July 12 was going to be special for the newlyweds, who graduated together from the Technion and were married ten months ago.  It was to be Udi’s last day of military reserve service. Karnit cooked his favorite meal as she listened to the news of his unit’s skirmish. “I sent him a message; after an hour he didn’t answer so I started calling.”  At 3:30 pm, four IDF officers entered her house, where family and friends gathered expectantly for his call.

 

“They told me Udi was either dead or kidnapped.”

 

Ehud turned 31 in captivity last week, but his birthday celebration went on as planned. She will show him the video. She wants people to know more about her “soulmate and the love of her life.” Udi recently earned a skipper’s license, and he loves photography, nature and biking.

 

“I get a lot of energy and strength from meeting you here,” Karnit told the attentive group. “We are going to bring Udi back home. The moon is coming closer and closer because of you.”

 

Surrounded by mission members as she left the stage, Karnit admired mission participant Iris Feinberg’s unusual necklace–a gold Lion of Judah pendant. She wondered why so many in the audience were wearing the same piece. “It’s beautiful,” she said. Iris explained that thousands of Jewish women around the world wear the Lion of Judah to show their love of the Jewish people. On an impulse, Iris unclasped the choker, placed it around Karnit’s neck, and said “We’re here with you, we’re standing behind you.”

 

Welcome and Shalom, Karnit Goldwasser. We are proud to call you a true Lion of Judah.

 

Karnit welcomes your wishes of support. Contact her at karnitgold@gmail.com

 

This experience has reinforced that we are one people, and when one part is injured, we are all injured.  Karnit’s strength is magnificent.

- Marc Borovoy, Detroit

 

The Israeli soldier is a part-time soldier, but a full time mensch and human being– perhaps an engineer, chemist, doctor, lawyer, composer–a person of great promise for the future.

- Terry Steen, Ventnor

 

Every resilient, strong, and determined Israeli we meet thanks us for being here and tells us it makes a difference. We need you now to help. It is our obligation and responsibility to keep Israel safe and secure. 

- Karen Alpiner, Detroit

 

This Shabbat when I light the candles I made with the Ethiopian Israeli children, I’ll think of the kids who went from war-torn Ethiopia to absorption centers to bomb shelters to these summer camps to be safe.

- Andrew Berger, Baltimore

 

I’ve been on 60-70 missions, and have been here during every war since 1973.  As tough as it is this week, there is not a person in this country that will let anyone interfere in their daily life.

- Jane Sherman, Detroit

 

 

Tuesday July 25, 2006 | Haifa, Israel

 

"Haifa Terror, Up Close"
by Harriet J. Dobin

Seventy American Jews will never forget where they were today.  Fox News called them the “Mercy Mission.”  Israeli Prime Minister Ehud Olmert personally praised them for “solidarity and support, carrying the full weight of the Jewish people.”  But the men and women who put their lives on hold for a week to fly to Israel will remember this as the day they came face to face with terror.

 

In a private noon session after his meeting with U.S. Secretary of State Condaleeza Rice, the UJC Solidarity Mission heard Israeli Prime Minister Olmert warn, Hezbollah “will have to pay, we are not running away.” [click here to read Prime Minister's Olmert 's remarks.]  Then they drove up the coast to Haifa, where they were eyewitnesses to the aftermath of a deadly Kaytusha rocket attack on Shalom Kassan Street. Escorted by Haifa Police chief Nir Maryash, UJC officials and a team of armed security men, the nervous mission members received instructions on what to do when a siren goes off and where to stand for shelter.

 

The rocket--one of 16 missiles Hezbollah fired at Haifa today--blew a crater in the side of a beige stucco apartment building behind a small shopping center in a middle-class neighborhood. The usually busy shopping center was deserted. The dentist, hairdresser, bank and shnitzel restaurant are closed for business during this week of violence. Two UJC mission busses arrived just hours after the attack, in time to view a shrapnel-torn silver car on a tow truck.  Shattered windows, crushed doors and hundreds of deadly holes were vicious reminders of the horrors Israeli civilians in the North have been confronting for two weeks.

 

Despite the violence, Israeli flags were hanging next to wet laundry on the window grates. Families waved and thanked mission members, who yelled “Am Yisrael chai!” (The people of Israel live) before being whisked back to the buses. They met with the mayor of Haifa and took a quick drive through the center of town–empty of regular traffic--before heading back to Tel Aviv, laden with images they will never forget.

 

How brave and inspirational the Israelis are. A Haifa resident told us not to worry, even after his building was hit.
- Sam Greenblatt, Philadelphia

 

The devastated vehicle within 200 yards of the missile looked like it was riddled by a machine gun—an effect caused by thousands of Kaytusha pellets. Please contact your Congressmen and call President Bush, 202-456-1111, and thank them for supporting Israel.

- Tim R. Cohen, Washington, D.C.

 

Haifa is a ghost town.

- Lainey Simonson, Philadelphia

 

My friend from Ranaana visited me before paying a shiva call on the family of a young soldier killed in the north. The rabbi who buried him performed his wedding three weeks ago.

- Elaine Schreiber, Phoenix

 

I came here to Haifa to see my friend Vered, whom I met through the Boston-Haifa partnership.  Her city is being bombed, and I came to support Israel and my friend.

- Stephanie Gertz, Boston

Miri Eisen, the press director from the Prime Minister’s office, taught us how we need to approach the press and public when we return. In Haifa we drove past Microsoft, Intel and Hewlett Packard at 3 pm, and the parking lots were deserted.

- Laura Green, Salt Lake City

 

Right and left, all are united in their support of Israel.

- Yisroel Schulman, New York

 

Driving through downtown Haifa with barely a car in sight, I only wished for a traffic jam. The spirit of the mayor left no doubt that the energy, vitality and growth of Haifa will resume. Bring on the cars.

- Noah Levine, Atlanta


The average citizen is determined, confident and resolute to change the paradigm where the people of Israel need to live with terrorists on their border who want to kill them.

- Steven Nasatir, Chicago

 

Miri Eisen, with her young son, told us, “We are empathetic, but we will not be apologetic.” Israel is strong, they will prevail. 

- Steven Lear, Minnesota


We went to the airport to welcome 640 new olim from France, in the middle of a war! What a happy, meaningful moment in the midst of heartache. How proud we are to be here at this time, inspired by today’s new olim.

Iris Feinberg, Atlanta
Susan Stern, New York
Etta Zimmerman, South Palm Beach County
Lynne Halpern, Atlanta
Beth Mann, New York City

Monday July 24, 2006 | Kfar Saba, Israel

"Israel's Teens Live Generously in Crisis"
by Harriet J. Dobin

"We are going to a center for displaced persons."

The Misson bus guide surely did not realize the ominous sound of his words. For 70 members of the UJC Solidarity Mission en route to Kfar Saba on Monday July 24, it was a frightening reality check. Mission members were in the eye of the storm on the third front in this war. A rippling humanitarian, economic and social crisis is taking aim at Israel, along with Hizbollah's Kaytusha rockets.

Factories, grocery stores, offices, nursery schools and health centers are shuttered in the North.

Families with means have long gone to hotels or overseas. But there are those who simply could not leave. JAFI has opened a trauma support hot line, 11,000 personal care kits were sent up north. 2,600 hot meals are served each day to shut-ins, because everything is closed. The AMEN Israeli teen volunteer corps has mobilized to pack activity kits and entertain children in JAFI summer camps, joined by Solidarity Mission members today. The mission was joined on Day Two by a Fox News crew from New York.

JDC's rescue and relief had come to mean aid in far-away countries with tongue-twister names. 

But this is not the former Soviet Union. This is Israel.

Almost 8,000 children - many of them Ethiopian Israeli children who arrived just last month - have been separated from parents. Israel Crisis Fund dollars are sending them to JAFI summer camps, and paying the unbudgeted salaries of staff members who cancelled vacations and were called back to work. Volunteers - ranging from teenagers to off-duty soldiers - who are helping in any way they can.

Israeli newspapers are running ads from families in central Israel opening up their homes to anyone from the North. Rishon L'Tzion is hosting 1,000 displaced Israelis. Herzliya is too. Hotels in Jerusalem and Tel Aviv are booking up fast. Galilee hotels are closed for business, unable to meet payroll, employees without wages. Departing El Al flights are full, but they return empty. Presidents of international corporation in the north are managing their companies inside a basement bomb shelter, if their employees who are still around can get to work safely.

The UJC Solidarity Mission heard it all on Day Two:

There’s no place like home. There’s no place like Israel.
- Karen Alpiner, Detroit

The Israeli spirit is alive and well, as we witnessed today in a teen volunteer program in Kfar Saba. This speaks loudly and positively for the future of Israel!
- Marc Borovoy, Detroit

Life is going on in Jerusalem as normally as can be. Kids are in parks, roller-blading and walking on the streets. People seem to be going about business as usual. It was very interesting that teens are using their summer vacations volunteering for kids in bomb shelters. They are very serious about their work.
- Rita Gann, Boston

I am meant to be visiting Israel at this moment in time. I thank my family for supporting me, all of them encouraged me to go without hesitation.
- Janine Sobel, Southern New Jersey

I met Neta, a 15 year old young girl who spends four days a week volunteering in Kfar Saba as a math tutor, packing boxes for kids trapped in bomb shelters. I am so proud of Israel?s young people.
- Ande Adelman, Philadelphia

So far, the spirit is strong, with concern for those immediately affected. I will take back images of the Ethiopian Israeli kids dancing Amharic rock with Lester!
- Midge Shafton, Chicago

The enthusiasm of the kids volunteering in the sweltering heat was contagious. This country is united like I have never seen it and it's wonderful to see such good things come out of these troubled times.
- Mark Levenson, Clifton-Passaic, New Jersey

Sunday July 23, 2006 | Jerusalem, Israel

“The family is in trouble and we’ve arrived”
by Harriet J. Dobin
Two million Israeli citizens – a third of the country’s population – went to sleep in bomb shelters tonight, as two busloads of American Jews arrived in Jerusalem from Ben Gurion Airport for a four day UJC Solidarity Mission. While Hezbollah rockets continued to attack Haifa and the north, more than 60 Federation leaders from 25 North American cities landed armed with nothing more than love, compassion and a heart-felt need to comfort the people of Israel.

They flew in from the big cities –Atlanta, Boston, Chicago, Detroit, New York and from Washington DC. The small cities sent people too - Omaha, Nashville, Tampa, Hartford and York, Pa.

They spoke in different accents but in one united voice:

I’ve been coming to Israel since 1960. Since the Six Day War, I’ve never seen the people of Israel as together as they are today. We are letting Israel know you are here. Thank you for dropping everything, canceling appointments, and leaving family behind. You can’t imagine what that says to every family in Israel.
- Jane Sherman, Detroit

Daily life has effectively stopped in the North. I found eerie silence in Nahariya last week, where we came across a 73-year-old Russian woman drenched with sweat trying to buy food for her pets. She was panicked, crying and traumatized. We saw a hand painted sign that said “Thank You soldiers for defending us”. And the mayor of Nahariya said: “We can absorb more damage, we’re ok, keep on going.” It’s not enough that we be impassioned, we have to act in a powerful way. Whatever it takes, we are together with the people of Israel.
- Joel Alperson, Omaha

This is about showing up. The family is in trouble, Israel is in trouble and we’ve arrived. Back home, we’ve held rallies and raised dollars. We are the lucky ‘shlichim’ able to be here on behalf our communities back home. Our Federations have always been about strengthening the Jewish future. This week, here, it’s all about strengthening the Jewish present.
- John Ruskay, New York

Thirty seconds after the call came, I knew I had to be on that plane and take a stand. We’re with Israel in the good times and the not so good times. 
- Marc Terrill, Baltimore

A few months ago I was at Ben Gurion Airport when we brought Ethiopian Jews home and reunited them with their families after 14 years. Arriving today I felt like I was going home and bringing all of my community with me.
- Iris Feinberg, Atlanta

I’m here to share my spirit and my music with the people of Israel:

“We are here, we are proud, we are strong and free.
And we’ll sing it to the sky.
We were then, we are now, we will always be
Am Yisrael Chai!”
Original music and lyrics by Shirav.
- Rabbi David Paskin, Canton Mass.
