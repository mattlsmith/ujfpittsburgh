Accepting the Challenge: A Rosh Chodesh Nisan Reflection

By Douglas Aronin
April 3, 2003


When I spent my junior year in Israel some twenty-five years ago (gulp!), I remember one of my professors inviting as a guest lecturer a Jewish woman who had recently arrived in Israel from what was then the Soviet Union.

In the course of a lengthy presentation about the state of Soviet Jewry at that time, she made one comment that has stayed with me over the years. Commenting on the frequency with which the slogan "Let My People Go" was used in the various Soviet Jewry rallies and marches that were common at that time, she chided us (as American Jews) for using a slogan that was incomplete: "The Torah doesn't just say shalach et ami -- 'Let My people go', but shalach et ami veyaavduni -- 'Let My people go that they may serve Me'" (Exodus 5:1).

Her words are particularly apt as we enter the final two weeks of preparation for Pesach, for there is no holiday on the Jewish calendar whose fundamental message is as routinely misunderstood as that which we denominate Zeman Cheiruteinu (the Season of our Freedom). The freedom that we celebrate on Pesach is not identical to the freedom we cherish as Americans. The freedom we were given when our people was liberated from Egyptian bondage was not the freedom to do whatever we want but rather the freedom to fulfill our potential in the service of God.

That distinction is the foundation on which the significance of Rosh Chodesh Nisan -- the first day of the month in which Pesach falls -- is built. "This month shall mark for you the beginning of months; it shall be the first of the months of the year for you" (Ex. 12:2, JPS translation). It is from the day of Rosh Chodesh Nisan that we as Jews mark time. It is from that moment that the calendar which distinguishes us as a people begins.

Yet what is the significance of Rosh Chodesh Nisan? If we wanted to begin counting time from the day of our redemption from Egyptian bondage, we would start with the fifteenth of the month, which is when that Redemption took place. If we wanted to begin counting from the day on which we as a people took the first concrete step to facilitate our redemption -- by designating and segregating the lambs that would become the first Passover offering -- then we would start from the tenth of Nisan, the day on which that act took place.

Why then should our calendar begin not from either of those days but rather from Rosh Chodesh Nisan?

Rosh Chodesh Nisan is the day on which God gave the first command to the Jewish people as a whole. Though the Sefer HaChinuch, the most popular of the works that seek to enumerate the 613 mitzvot, lists three commandments that precede it in the Torah (the commands to reproduce, to circumcise and to refrain from eating the hollow of an animal's thigh), Rosh Chodesh Nisan marks the first day on which God, through Moses, gave a command that was addressed to the entire Jewish people.  So significant was that event that some commentators debated why the Torah did not start from that point.

With the start of Nisan, we suspend the saying of tachanun, the daily prayer for forgiveness, until the beginning of the next month, a practice that many erroneously assume to be connected with our preparations for the upcoming holiday. Actually, the reason for suspending tachanun at the beginning of Nisan has nothing to do with the events of Pesach per se, but rather is connected to an event that occurred the following year, the setting up of the mishkan (portable sanctuary) and the inauguration of the daily sacrifice (Exodus 40:17).

It would seem then that the significance of Rosh Chodesh Nisan is twofold. It marks the day on which God first commanded the Jewish people to serve Him rather than our Egyptian masters. And it marks the day, one year later, when the Jewish people began to make the service of God a part of their daily lives.

In between those two events, of course, a lot happened: the exodus from Egypt, the crossing of the sea, the war against Amalek, the Revelation at Sinai and the sin of the golden calf, just to name some of the highlights.

Yet after what may well be the most eventful year in the history of the world, the Jewish people came back to the essence of Rosh Chodesh Nisan. On that first Rosh Chodesh Nisan, God challenged us to dedicate our national life to His service. On the same day, one year later, the Jewish people accepted His challenge. And from that day forward, the essence of Jewish history has been about our struggle to live up to the challenge that our ancestors accepted.

May the challenge of Nisan inspire us to continue that struggle so as to bring ourselves, and the world, one step closer to God's ultimate Redemption.

Chodesh tov.
