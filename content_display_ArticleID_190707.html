"The Partnership Report"- An On The Scene Bulletin From Karmiel & Misgav

July 31, 2006


To our dear friends and partners,

Today is Monday, the 20th day of the war. A "partial 48-hour cessation of air activity" went
A Musical Interlude for Misgav Employees in one of the Council's Shelters during a Katyusha Rocket Attack
into effect during the early hours of the morning. Throughout the day and until now as I write we've had, thankfully no siren warnings or incoming rockets in the area. It is of course too early to say that the war has ended, however the temporary quiet has given us the opportunity to get some fresh air and prepare for what might come next?

The Home Front Command instructions still require us to remain in shelters or "secure rooms"; however, after 20 long days of anxiety and apprehension, residents have begun to leave their shelters for limited periods during those "golden" moments of quiet.

So What's The State Of The "State Of Emergency" In The Region?

Misgav
The council is continuing its efforts to stock the shelters with all of the necessary equipment, provide organized activities for children and teenagers in the various secure zones, organize various activities outside the region for our youth, conduct visits to the various communities and meet with the region's residents and community leadership.

The "With You From Here to the North" Food Campaign Came to the Misgav Region
Supersal Food Shipments for Misgav Communities

Some 220 boxes of food, dry goods (shampoo, canned goods, pasta and sugar, etc) and baby products that were collected through contributions made by residents from throughout the country at various "Supersal" supermarkets were sent this week to the region.

According to Ayleet Barnea Nesher, Misgav's Volunteer Unit Coordinator the boxes will be distributed to needy Misgav families starting today.

Ayleet also noted that the council received a number of gift packages from Police Cadets Company 542 of the Israel Police Training Academy in Kiryat Ata. The packages, along with notes written by the cadets were distributed to children and families in communities throughout the region. Some of the children responded with thank you notes of their own and others even telephoned the police academy to say thanks. One of the cadets, a young woman wrote, "To the families of the north, please take care of yourselves and be strong. Most importantly, "keep the faith" and smile. I hope that the war in the north will end soon and all of us will be able to return to our homes."


Another welcomed initiative is that of the Misgav branch of the Israel Soldier's Welfare Association to provide gift packages to our IDF soldiers in the hope that security and quiet will be restored and they will all return home safe and healthy. ISWA volunteers and others from the Volunteer Unit are currently organizing the distribution of these packages to IDF soldiers in the field. The volunteers turned to Misgav residents to join them in the effort to gather various products needed by our soldiers over the coming days. The intention is to collect contributions of cakes, candies, toilet articles (soap & shampoo), underwear, radios and CD's, etc. The items will be collected in each community and then brought to council offices where there they will be packaged and shipped to the soldiers.

Karmiel
The municipality continues to meet the city's daily needs. This includes the continued installation of air-conditioners in the shelters as well as providing them with all of the
Education Commander Brig. Gen Ilan Harari meeting with Adi Eldar
necessary equipment, organizing children's activities, distributing hot meals and food products to the elderly and just running the city as usual. Today representatives of the Joint Distribution Committee (JDC), Dr. Yigal Ben Shalom, Director General of the National Insurance Institute, New York State Assemblyman Dov Hikind and a delegation of supporters from New York as well as Brigadier General Ilan Harari, Commander of the IDF Education Corp visited the city. The visitors were all met by the mayor who reported on the city's needs and the wonderful work done by the teacher-soldiers sent by the army to organize activities for the children in the shelters.

Avi Hilva, the volunteer chairman of the Israel Soldier's Welfare Association Karmiel Branch, met the mayor today and received his support for the association's distribution of gift packages to Karmiel's young people who have begun their required army service and because of the war were unable to attend the traditional party sponsored every year by the city and the ISWA. 

Until today, my reports have largely stuck to factual reporting and a presentation of what's happening to us, the regions residents. Today I decided to report about a welcomed initiative sponsored by the "Karmi Hai" Association in Karmiel, the city's Veterinary Service and the City of Karmiel. These three organizations came together in the first days of the war to provide a much needed support network of the city's pets. The sudden outbreak of warfare and the massive rocket attacks on the city forced many residents to leave their homes hastily, including many families who over the years have faithfully raised a variety of cats and dogs. 

Many families were forced to leave their pets at home and thereby creating a situation in which large numbers of abandoned, frightened, hungry and thirsty animals are wandering the city.

The Veterinary Service, in cooperation with the "Karmi Hai" Association and the city of Karmiel has begun to locate, assemble and treat these abandoned pets. A "holding and treatment" facility was prepared in the city's industrial park. Dogs with biometric ID chip undergo an owner ID and location process to re-unite the pet with their displaced owners. Dogs without the chip are cared for temporarily in the holding facility and efforts are made to find them suitable "foster families".

The care of cats on the other hand is limited to the operation of a number of feeding stations throughout the city. "Karmi Hai" volunteers make sure that there is a constant and sufficient supply of cat food and fresh water for the city's cats.

Dr. Israel Weiss, the city's veterinarian stated that to the best of his knowledge, two pets (a cat and a dog) were killed in a Katyusha rocket attack. He also noted that many pets have begun to show serious stress-related symptoms such as enlarged pupils, shivers, and unstable behavior such as running crazily about and constantly searching for hiding places, etc. Some of the animals required specialized medical care in order to calm them down, similar to people.

It seems that the emergency affects not only humans and it is important of all of us to consider this and pay attention to this sector of the population as well.

Interestingly enough, the aliyah to Israel continues, even in times such as these. The following was written by the Woodward family who has recently moved to Karmiel from their home in South Africa. The following is a recounting of their most recent experiences in their new home.  

Notes from Israel
"We flew out from South Africa on 8 July 2006 on an overnight flight to Israel. We landed at Ben Gurion International Airport near Tel Aviv at about 5AM SA time
Tony, Brenda & Kaeleigh Woodward at the Karmiel Absorption Center in Karmiel
which is six in the morning Israeli time. We were taken through to the immigration department where they loaded all the relevant information they needed from us straight into the computer. Much to our surprise, we were issued a joint temporary ID document and our first lot of money straight away. From there they took us to collect our luggage and through customs. Once that was all complete they organized us a taxi and we left on our first trip in Israel. All this took only about 2 hours as we were on our way just before 08h00 Israeli time.

The taxi trip was both exciting and a bit of a nightmare, as we were seeing a new country for the first time as well as experiencing a new style of driving, i.e. driving on the right side of the road and drivers that are a lot worse than you find in the good old SA. The traffic in Tel Aviv is just as you might find in Johannesburg, and we were stuck in it. When we did eventually get to leaving Tel Aviv (we had to pick up a young lady in the centre and drop her off at a kibbutz just outside Tel Aviv), we would settle down to a lot of traffic, but with a changing scenery as we traveled.

Even though Karmiel is only about 160 km’s from Tel Aviv we only arrived at around 11h30.We were shown to our new home (1 bedroom flatlet ) and with the help of a couple of youngsters that were outside we got all our luggage up to our flat on the 2nd floor. When Brenda took one look at all this, she just sat down and cried. This was due to a combination of the size of the flat, tiredness as we had not been able to sleep on the plane, and the heat we endured from Tel Aviv to Karmiel. It is a lot cooler in Karmiel.

After she had had a short nap we went down to meet Ziona who is the director of the absorption centre in Karmiel. From the time we landed in Israel, we have come across people who care.  It does not matter where you go; the Jewish people stick together and always care about each other.

We had only 3 days to discover our city, which we have not fully discovered. We walked everywhere and we were truly amazed at the little shops and all the things that you can buy here.

We could not wait; we had our first Falafel with the wonderful tasty radishes that are served here. We are totally hooked on the radishes.  The hummus is fantastic, and we cannot go a day without eating it with something.

Well our first experience was on Wednesday, 12 July, when after 2 days of paperwork and government departments we decided we were now able to catch the bus to the shopping centre ourselves.  We had hardly arrived at the shopping centre when the first Katyushas hit.  Coming from South Africa, we are really green, and had no idea what happened.  Everyone was running and shouting, and when you are in new country you don’t speak the language, worse you had no idea the danger you were in.  Well finally, someone told us to evacuate. We then heard that a bomb had fallen in the shopping centre 1 minute away. That was really nerve wrecking.  Well we got a bus to stop and pick us up to go, which felt like the longest bus trip of my life for about 1 km only.

Since the 12 July, all we have basically known is the Mercaz Klita, and the bomb shelter.  We spend a lot of time in the latter.  On the 22 July, we experienced something horrific. At about 16h30, we were sitting and watching TV like normal in our flatlet, and Brenda heard a Katyusha land, she immediately commented we must move and before we all had the chance the second one landed.  I just shouted at them to lie down and pushed them into the bedroom.  The third Katyusha landed 10m from our bedroom window, the emotional anxiety, stress and fear cannot be described. The blast was extremely loud, and landed in the road, hurting people in two cars and destroying the cars.  Our building on the side of the road did not have a window that was not broken.  The security guard’s car was parked on the side of the building lost it’s back window.  We only thank G-d that no one in our building was hurt.

The next day friends of ours phoned from Ra’anana and asked us to come and spend time there. We went for a week.  Ra’anana is incredibly hot and if you don’t enjoy the heat then you will be very uncomfortable there, and will find yourself in an air-conditioned building or room most of the time.

We got back to Karmiel on 28 July, and we were hardly back 30 minutes and we were back in the bomb shelters periodically.  Our experience here can truly be described as a baptism of fire.

We are looking forward to this war ending so that our life can start and our daughter Kaeleigh who is 14 can start school.  She has been very bored and the centre that we are at and does not have anyone who speaks English, so she has been very lonely.  She is dying to start school and make new friends. 

Although this has been quite an unstable start for us, we are still determined to stay in Israel and make it our home."

Tony, Brenda and Kaeleigh Woodward.

That's all for today, let us pray that quiet will be restored and peace and security will return to the region and the country.


Best Regards,

Yael Raich
