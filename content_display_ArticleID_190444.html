"The Partnership Report"- An On The Scene Bulletin From Karmiel & Misgav

July 30, 2006


To our dear friends and partners,
Shell Damage to a Memorial Plague at ORT Cramim

Today is Sunday, the 19th day of the war. Overall, we had a "relatively" quiet weekend in the region, with only a few rounds of Katyusha rockets striking our area. On Friday we began the day with three sirens, one after the other, fortunately they were not followed by the noise of exploding rockets in the region. This changed during the early afternoon when a number of rockets struck Karmiel near the campus of the ORT Horvitz High School and the giant amphitheatre in which we hold the International Dance Festival every July. Because of the current situation, the festival was postponed and rescheduled for October.

The morning hours of Shabbat were quiet but towards the afternoon, a number of rockets fell in various public areas throughout Karmiel. Today we began the day with a siren alert that ordered the region's residents to find shelter in a bomb shelter or secure zone. Fortunately, we received no reports of rockets falling in either Karmiel or Misgav.

So What's The State Of The "State Of Emergency" In The Region? Karmiel
During the current situation, the municipality, which is responsible for the care and maintenance for over 30 public bomb shelters decided to add to its responsibilities the care
Adi Eldar, Sammy Segol (Keter Plastic) and Karmiel Children in a shelter during a rocket attack on the city
and maintenance of those shelters found in the basements of apartment buildings throughout the city although the responsibility for these shelters is legally that of the buildings' residents.

This decision was reached after understanding that the continued "State of Emergency" made it impossible for residents to overcome the inherent difficulties and maintain the proper conditions for continued use of these shelters.  Today, the city is caring for some 400 shelters and making every effort to improve conditions for staying in the shelters through the installation of air-conditioners and/or fans, televisions, emergency lighting, mattresses, games and food, etc.

This past Friday an emergency meeting of the "15" Forum (a gathering of mayors of Israel's 15 largest cities) was held in Haifa under the leadership of Adi Eldar (Adi, as well as Mayor of Karmiel also serves as chairman of the Israel Local Authorities Union). On behalf of the northern municipalities, Adi thanked the mayors of the south and central regions for the warm home hospitality and other assistance provided to families and youth groups from the north and asked that this continue until the end of the current emergency.

Today, Offer Eini, chairman of the Histadrut Labor Union visited the city and heard about the hardships facing employees forced to miss work for the past 3 weeks.

Misgav
Unloading of a Mobile Shelter
Following an urgent request from Erez Kriesler, mayor of the Misgav Regional Council, the Ministry of Defense provided six mobile bomb shelters to two adjoining Misgav communities, Kibbutz Eshbal and the Bedouin village of Arab Al Na'im. The council asked that these temporary means be provided to the two communities since both are without shelters and/or secure zones.
Placement of a Mobile Shelter

The mobile shelters arrived on semi-trailers and with the assistance of the Maintenance and Engineering Departments of the Regional Council, plots were made suitable for their placement in the center of each of the communities. Each community received three shelters; each of which is approximately 100 sq. ft.

The council has completed its distribution of 12 sets of electronic goods and games contributed to the children of the Misgav region by Bank

Hapoalim.

The Bedouin village of Salameh happily accepted the set for use in the village's elementary school, where the children gather daily for organized activities.

Monir Suad, chairman of the village's village council noted that all of the day camps operating in the village were cancelled because of the security situation and instead daily activities have been organized in the elementary where there is a suitable bomb shelter.

In addition to Salameh, the council also distributed the sets in additional communities located north of Hillzone such as Tuval, Har Halutz, Lavon, Harashim, Tel Al, Tsurit, Camun, Michmanim, Kmana, Moran and Lotam.  

The council also distributed games and arts & crafts sets to all of the communities in the region that were contributed by the JDC and the Ministry of Education.

The following is the second of two reports from Shuki Levinger, Executive Director of Kishorit. Kishorit is a permanent framework for adults with special needs.
Dear Friends,
I haven't written to you for about a week but now I have a chance to update you about life in Kishorit over the past few days:
We had a difficult time deciding about evacuation. The road south is the road to safety; on the other hand, we can only move away from Kishorit as a community, with the full medical and psychological contingent in tow. Dispersing individual members around the country won’t work. However, with all the hotels in the center and south of Israel being full to capacity with families from the north, the option of moving together doesn’t exist. Besides, our members don’t want to move away from familiar territory, which is understandable.

The shriek of sirens blasting through Kishorit on an average of one an hour throughout the day unnerves our members to such an extent that we have introduced our own, gentler version: the telephone. Now, even if you’re in the midst of a phone conversation, a human voice will cut through, asking you to leave whatever you are doing and seek shelter immediately. To guarantee that everyone “gets the message,” a member of staff circulates the kibbutz, bullhorn in hand, like an old-fashioned town crier. It seems to work beautifully. 
  
On the whole, most of our members are coping, though there are exceptions. Panic attacks are on the increase. Other than supplementing medication, we prescribe a shoulder to cry on and a hug – we have plenty of both. Also, a growing number require round-the-clock supervision, so we have assigned to each a “mentor” who stays by them during the day and sleeps beside them at night.
 
Some of our problems are shared by thousands of others in the north while some are unique to us: Our “mentoring scheme” has been offered to all our members who also have physical disabilities. They now have someone to accompany and assist them wherever they go.

One member cannot join the rest of us in the shelters because he is afraid to be with other people in close quarters. So we are constructing a security room attached to his apartment, which he can easily reach. We hope it will solve the problem. Work was begun two days ago, and we expect completion in five more.

As for the shelters, we already understand that they are far too crowded for our members (with 2 sq.m. per person there is little more than legroom) and we will have to build two new, larger shelters after this war. These will also have to include showers, which we don’t have in our current ones. Trying to rely on the previous day’s “schedule” of Katyushas, we are urging members to take showers during anticipated quieter moments. But we are not always accurate in our assessment of Hezbollah programming, and terrified members are finding themselves in the middle of a shower, knowing full-well that it will be a real challenge to get dressed, out of their apartments and into a shelter in time.
A major problem has been Katyushas exploding in fields and causing brush fires. With many falling in our area, the possibility of a fire raging throughout the kibbutz is a serious worry. Earlier this week we called in a company that brought with it heavy machinery and plowed a fire lane right around us.
And so it goes; more than anything else, we are all very tired but hopeful that things get easier soon.
Yours,
 
Shuki

 
That's all for today, let us pray that quiet will be restored and peace and security will return to the region and the country.


Shabbat Shalom,

Yael Raich
