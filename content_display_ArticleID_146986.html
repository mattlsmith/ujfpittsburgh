Shai From Jerusalem: Why I Voted for Mehereta

By Nachman Shai
Thi story originally appeared in The Jerusalem Post.
March 6, 2005


The last episode of the reality television show "The Ambassador" had unusually high ratings. Thirty-seven percent of all TV viewers in Israel -- approximately 1.5 million viewers -- watched the episode broadcast during peak viewing hours.

Though the ratings of the other segments were a bit lower, some reached around 30%, which is also a record.

This means, first of all, that the audience loved the show. It also means that the TV critics, who didn't miss a chance to blast it, don't necessarily speak for the public.

Of the three judges on the program I was the only one who voted for Mehereta Baruch, a young Israeli from Ethiopia to be "the Ambassador" -- to actually work for a private group, Israel at Heart, in New York.

Reactions to my vote have been sizable and mixed. Some claimed that rather than choosing the best person for the job, my choice was a pure case of affirmative action toward the Ethiopian-Israeli community. An equal number supported my recommendation based on their own assessment of Mehereta's skills and personality, as they had judged them over the course of the show.

The over 100,000 Ethiopian Jews who live here are a unique community with particular characteristics. Their families tend to be large -- over 85% comprise more than five members. The community is overwhelmingly young, about half of its numbers teens and children. Two-thirds of the community live in permanent homes, about half in 15 peripheral areas of the country.

Unemployment in the community is high, leading to a situation in which many children lack basic school equipment, help in doing homework and more. It is not surprising that parents preoccupied with the problem of finding a way to earn money have trouble providing for their children's educational and other needs.

Such difficulties quickly translate into poor school achievement, low high-school matriculation rates, and low university attendance. A survey made in 2000 of Hebrew and math achievement found that Israelis of Ethiopian origin in the 8th and 9th grades had scored just 60-70% of the results of their peers. By 11th grade, these figures had deteriorated even further, to 40-60%.

Focusing on education is the right -- in fact only -- way to absorb Ethiopian Jews in Israel. We must recognize this, while admitting that this means integrating adults will be even harder, and perhaps less successful.

Mehereta Baruch represents the young generation of Ethiopian immigrants. She arrived here at age 10, after a long journey in the desert, and has lived here for 21 years. She is an actress. She received a bachelor's degree at a university here and is studying for her masters degree. She is married to Eran, a pilot.

Her Hebrew is excellent; her English, reasonable. She knows and loves Israel. Her knowledge of it is not perfect, but she catches up easily and will succeed in this, too.

I watched her on the show, overcoming each and every hurdle and reaching the finals. It was obvious to me that she could succeed, here and in New York.

She is most definitely a good representative of those immigrants who have come to Israel from all corners of the globe; but more, she is the model of a young, able person with a very Israeli drive to evolve, develop and break free.

The "Ethiopian National Project" that the government has decided upon is being implemented, albeit slowly and gradually. But at least it is on the right track toward easing the substantial distress of this community. Alongside it are many projects -- some governmental, others by the Jewish Agency for Israel and the American Joint Distribution Committee -- and others initiated by nongovernmental organizations within the Ethiopian community.

Success stories like Mehereta Baruch's obviously cannot substitute for these projects. She can, however, complement them by being a visible example of someone who immigrated, was absorbed in the community succeeded and will continue to do so.

Mehereta did not win "The Ambassador" contest. But luckily, and despite what is called "reality TV," we feel the real world may recognize her skills more fully.

I am confident that she will go on to represent Israel with loyalty and skill in some capacity, and that all of us, newcomers and old-timers, will be the better for it.

The writer is the director general of the United Jewish Communities, Israel; and was a judge on the TV program "The Ambassador."
