"The Partnership Report"- An On-The-Scene Bulletin From Karmiel & Misgav

July 19, 2006


To our dear friends and partners,

Thankfully last night passed with no significant damage or injuries. During the day, however, renewed shelling severely damaged a home in Karmiel and fell in an open area adjacent to a Karmiel home for senior citizens. All the remaining rockets fell in open areas in both Karmiel and Misgav. In addition, more residents temporarily left the region, moving to safer quarters in the central or southern sectors of the country.

Local authorities are well organized and operating in a crisis management format to provide basic municipal services to the region's remaining residents and respond to any immediate emergency.

Local health services have restored critical emergency services throughout the region. Postal services will be restored in the coming days. Supermarkets are also open in coordination with security forces to allow those residents remaining in the region to leave the bomb shelters and/or secure rooms for brief periods to purchase food and other supplies.

Under the auspices of the Karmiel and Misgav Community Centers, volunteers from the region and throughout the country have initiated a series of organized activities for groups of children, who, because of the situation, have been forced to remain in shelters and/or secure rooms. These activities include arts & crafts, music, games, films and television shows as well as performances by many well-known Israeli performers and artists to relieve the daily stress and help the children cope with the difficult and frightening situation at hand.

Companies and organizations from throughout the country are generously contributing to local municipalities. The "Tnuva" Dairy Company graciously contributed 800 cartons of UHT Long Life Milk and 2,400 cartons of UHT Chocolate Milk. Bank Leumi contributed backpacks and other companies contributed toys and games.

It is during these difficult times that the Jewish People and the citizens of the State of Israel shine in their fullest splendor.

Today I have decided to add something of a more personal nature to our daily report. It is an article published in today's Ha'aretz written by Ron Shapiro, a resident of Mitzpe Aviv (Misgav) describing his feelings and experiences during the current hostilities.

 
"The Secure Room Diary" Ron Shapiro
Corn? Who Buys Corn on a Day Like This?
Mitzpe Aviv
It was just after 10PM, but the end of a very long day that actually began the night before with Katyusha rockets falling on Afula, Migdal Haemek and Givat Ella. Once more, the rockets were not targeted at us; rather they passed over on their way a few miles to the south. Nevertheless, 24 hours later the eyelids were unable to hold the load. After only a few paragraphs, my eyes simply closed.

Then, after dozing off a few minutes, the air-raid siren was sounded. Should we get out of bed and trudge over to the secure, bombproof room? After considering the matter for a few seconds, a positive decision was reached. In the meantime, someone drove through our community with a megaphone calling out for all of the residents of Mitzpe Aviv to enter their secure rooms.

The neighbors told us that that's how it was for the rest of the day. Twice or three times the alarm was sounded and everyone headed for their "secure rooms".

According to instructions from the community's security coordinator and the regional council's security department when the alarm is sounded everyone is required to enter their secure rooms for at least 20 minutes and only then they can leave the room (but stay within the confines of their house) if nothing has happened or if no other announcement is made.

After the next alarm, we headed directly to the secure room, just as we had been trained to do, almost second nature. By Friday other thoughts arose, like should we clear out all the "stuff" that had gathered in the secure room over the past five, six years? The smallest room in the house, which had only been built to meet building regulations set by the Home Front Command or Civil Defense codes, served as a storage room, was suddenly required to fulfill its intended purpose. Was the room ready? Were we?

It wasn't the room that supplied the answer, nor we. Nor were the Katyushas that fell on Majd Al Crum, Nahariya or Karmiel sufficiently convincing. It was only the rocket that fell in the Stella Mares neighborhood of Haifa that Friday evening that drove us to clear out all the old boxes, carpets and other stuff and arrange the room with some rugs and mattresses spread out on the floor, just so we would know that we are ready.

The two younger ones, graduates of 1st and 4th grades respectively, herein known as "the girls", even slept there overnight between Saturday and Sunday, just to be on the safe side. However as daybreak came, at the beginning of a new week it seemed that we wouldn't have to change our plans. They got up, got dressed, brushed their teeth, packed sandwiches and headed off to their day camp. Their mother drove to work in Haifa, near the "Check Post". The older one, almost 11, had an orthodontist appointment during the mid hours of the morning that required one of the parents to stay home, check the Internet and make some phone calls, until it was time to go to the dental clinic. Suddenly, shortly after 9:00AM, a dull boom or two was heard.

Later, we learned that this was the attack on the train garage in Haifa Bay.

From the house in Mitzpe Aviv, a 15 minute ride west to the Krayot and 20-25 minutes north to Karmiel, it was far, but just loud enough to get the really oldest one, the one who just two weeks ago completed 12th grade, out of bed.

Nonetheless to the dental clinic we must go. As we left the day camp, two concerned mothers asked if we had decided to take the girls home. No, no, we were on our way to an orthodontist appointment that had been set a long time ago. On our way back, by that time it was almost 11AM and all of the kids were headed for the shelters after receiving instructions to this effect from the regional council, but no one had yet considered canceling the day camp. Therefore, the daughter was off to day camp in the shelter and dad on his way home.

A few moments later the girls' mother came in with the two of them in hand. From her office at the clinic, the boom from the bay attack was heard much more clearly and served as a call to action. Anyway, some time passed between the time the employees, physicians and patients decided what had to be done in this situation and her decision that there was no value in waiting until the next incoming rocket attack, and that it was preferable to return home. On the way back, she recounted with an astonishment that didn't lessen even many hours later, how she saw quite a few people stop at makeshift roadside stands to buy fresh corn. Corn! Who could understand this? A Katyusha rocket could fall on them at any moment, and all they could think about was corn, fresh corn.

By Monday, the next day, day camp was canceled until further notice. Something in our routine had changed. Many preferred not going to work; rather they wished to work from home. There were those who decided that it was time to visit their relatives in the central and southern sections of the country, or like our girls, reserve a tzimmer in a calmer location. Anyways, a lot of rockets fell all around us, from Karmiel by way of Akko and the Kriyot, to Haifa, Givat Ella and Migdal Haemek.

By us, the southern most community of the Misgav Regional Council, between Aibleen and Tamra, not yet. The closest we came was in the bored mind of the young man who manned the command station in one of the nearby communities, and who bragged to his friends that a night ago, or two, or three, who remembers actually, a Katyusha fell on Mitzpe Aviv. After a few phone calls, the rumor that had spread through the region at the speed of sound was put to rest.

Yesterday as well, on Tuesday, the secure room remained vacant and useless, until about 5PM, when suddenly the booms returned, and with them, the sirens.

That's all for today. Let us pray that quiet will be restored and peace and security return to the region and the country.

Sincerely yours,
Yael Raich
