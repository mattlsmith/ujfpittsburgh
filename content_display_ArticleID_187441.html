CPAC Meeting Minutes June 1, 2006



Mahnaz Harrison presided as chair.

Also present: Karen Egorin, Lynne Frank, Mark Frank, Cindy Goodman-Leib, Rabbi James Gibson, Dave Gritzer, Marshall Hershberg, Lynette Lederman, Carolyn Mendelson, Barry Nathan, Sahar Oz, Mayda Roth, Roz Sherman, Aryeh Sherman, Hanna Steiner, Aaron Weil, Bob Whitehill, Dan Wiseman; Guests: Councilman Bill Peduto; State Representative Michael Gerber; UJF Staff: Jeffrey Cohan, CPAC director; Deborah Baron, director, Internal Operations/Agency Relations; Jeff Finkelstein, president/CEO; Hank Karp, director of marketing communications; Lisa Premo, CPAC administrative assistant.

Welcome and introductions
Mahnaz welcomed everyone and Carolyn Mendelson and Dan Wiseman, new members of CPAC, introduced themselves. Also introduced were Sahar Oz, new assistant director of the Hillel JUC and Representative Mike Gerber, a freshman in the legislature.

Remarks
Jeff Finkelstein reported that we have received more than $4 million from the government and more than $11 million from the campaign. He congratulated Chairwoman Harrison on being named co-chair of the 2007 JCPA Plenum. He then thanked her for her three years as chairwoman of CPAC and presented her with a Rosenstein print titled "Creation," paid for by CPAC members. .

VOTE on endorsing U.S. Israel Energy Cooperation Act
Jeff C. asked for approval to send a letter to Senators Specter and Santorum and our Representatives to co-sponsor this Act. He also encouraged members to write as individuals in support of it. Unanimously approved.

Report on Citizens Hearing on Health Care Reform
Rabbi Gibson reported that more than 300 people attended this hearing at Temple Sinai in support of HR 676, the U.S. National Health Insurance Act, also known as Expanded and Improved Medicare for All, introduced by Congressman John Conyers (D-Michigan). Congressman Mike Doyle (D-Swissvale), a former representative of the insurance industry, came to the hearing as an opponent of HR676 but changed to become a co-sponsor of it. Also noteworthy was the absence of any news coverage by the Post-Gazette either before or after the hearing.

Chairwoman Harrison thanked everyone for their involvement over the past three years. She plans to stay involved in CPAC.

Darfur update
Darfur rally and next steps
Marshall acknowledged Councilman Peduto for his participation in the Darfur rally. More than 275 Pittsburghers travelled in five buses to Washington, DC on April 30. CPAC helped cover the cost of the Million Voices for Darfur Postcard Campaign. The campaign continues via the web site www.millionvoicesfordarfur.org to let the administration know we support a strengthened peacekeeping force in Darfur.

Since the rally, a peace treaty has been reached between the government and leading rebel group. Two of the other groups including one of the rebel tribes voted against the treaty. Negotiations continue but are complicated by relationships between Sudan's government forces and its militia with Russia and China.

A FY2006 appropriations bill includes funding for Darfur. Future strategy includes letter writing to continue these efforts. The Pittsburgh Darfur Emergency Coalition (PDEC) is an organized local effort. The American Jewish World Service (AJWS) is coordinating the national focus.

Aaron recognized Jeff C. for contributing CPAC funds that helped Hillel Jewish University Center (JUC) students attend the rally. Students Taking Action Now (STAND) is a student effort at Carnegie Mellon University and the University of Pittsburgh started by 8 students at Hillel JUC. It has expanded to 120 students.

Jeff C. acknowledged Marshall and the great job he has done as liaison between PDEC and CPAC and in framing the issue.

Jeff C. said that a proposal has come forth asking CPAC to allocate funds for American Jewish World Service (AJWS). There is money from the CPAC programming budget that was not spent. He recommends that equal parts of any contribution go to support both the national (AJWS) and local efforts (PDEC) and made a motion for giving $150 to each. The motion was approved.

Specter's and Santorum's involvement
Chairwoman Harrison met with the chiefs of staff for Sens. Specter and Santorum after the Darfur Rally. She was there as a member of the national Commission on Social Action and Chair of CPAC. There were nine people from across Pennsylvania from all walks of life including evangelical churches. A woman from the evangelical churches made a point to Senator Santorum's Chief of Staff that 500 lives are lost each day in the Darfur region. This number is sure to rise now that the food ration from the U.N. World Food Program has been cut by half. The woman had voted for Senator Santorum because he is for life and with 500 lives being lost everyday, she wants to see him taking a leadership role on this. She and her churches will be watching and marching with their feet to vote or not vote for him in November depending on what he does. Eight to ten Darfurians who have resettled in Houston were flown in to Washington by the Religious Action Center (RAC) and the Commission on Social Action. They met with Senator Frist for 40 minutes. A supplemental bill is still sitting in the Senate. Activists should keep the pressure on the Senate and White House switchboards on this issue.

Status of Darfur refugees
Bob Whitehill reported that there are 70,000 refugees admitted to the U.S. this year in contrast to 54,000 last year. In the future, this number is likely to drop drastically to 25,000. A deterrent to immigration is the Real ID Act, a companion to the Patriot Act, which contains a provision precluding immigrants from coming if they have provided "material support" to a terrorist group. This provision is being enforced as "any support".

Bob said there is one escape route for Darfurian immigrants and that is from Darfur to Egypt to Israel. HIAS is trying to transfer refugees to the United States. The Jewish Family & Children's Service Refugee Program, Jewish Community and HIAS are working together.

Guest speaker Pittsburgh City Councilman Bill Peduto
Councilman Peduto recounted his previous visit to CPAC at which he met Senator Jane Orie. It was that connection that helped him work with her on Act 47.

On the issue of Darfur, Councilman Peduto explained that the reason for City Council involvement is that change has always begun at the local level. For a movement to be sustainable, it needs roots.

Councilman Peduto wants to help guide Pittsburgh’s new economy while preserving the neighborhoods which make up the district. He supports a more transparent government that utilizes technology to provide more efficient services for less money. He has advanced policies that provide more resources to improve neighborhood corridors through infrastructure upgrades, instead of grants to individual companies.

He would like greater funding of public transit to build a countywide light rail system that would connect with a regional commuter rail system.

Councilman Peduto describes himself as an independent Democrat who believes that policy should outweigh special interests.

Aaron asked about the impending casinos and Councilman Peduto's general outlook on the casino and its location. The Councilman replied that the casino will not be a panacea. The location of the casino is an urban planning issue. Locating it at Station Square would create traffic gridlock but not economic growth. Locating a casino in a buffer zone between the Hill and Downtown in the arena area has potential for new economic growth.

Barry asked about regionalization of water and sewage. Allegheny County has 18 sewer companies and 12 water companies.

Councilman Peduto responded that there is a new model of economic development and people are working together for sewer redevelopment. The Southwestern Pennsylvania Regional Transit Authority (SPARTA) is working on a Transit Vision Plan.

Meeting adjourned.
