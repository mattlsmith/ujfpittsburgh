AT 2006 UJC/FEDERATION GENERAL ASSEMBLY IN L.A., NETANYAHU LIKENS IRAN TO NAZI GERMANY  

 

Nov. 13, 2006
 

Israel's Likud Party leader, Benjamin "Bibi" Netanyahu, likened the radical Islamist government of Iran to Nazi Germany on the eve of World War II, as he delivered the closing plenary address on the second day of the 2006 General Assembly of United Jewish Communities/the Federations of North America in Los Angeles on Monday.

"It’s 1938, and Iran is Germany, and it’s racing to arm itself with nuclear weapons. Same tendencies: to slander and vilify its victim in preparation for slaughter," Netanyahu said. Iranian President Mahmoud "Ahmedinijad takes his cue from Hitler, and no one cares. Every week he talks about erasing Israel from the map, and no one says anything. While Ahmedinijad is denying the Holocaust, he is preparing another Holocaust for the Jewish state."

Netnayahu joined the growing chorus of Israeli government voices delivering stinging attacks on Iran during the GA. During Sunday's opening plenary keynote, Foreign Minister Tzipi Livni urged the international community to confront Iran.

Netanyau echoed those warnings as well. "Iran’s nuclear program goes well beyond the destruction of Israel. It’s a global program in the service of a mad ideology that’s been simmering in a corner of Islam for centuries: That history as we know -- the rise of the West -- it is a mistake that must be corrected. They don’t hate the West because of Israel -- they hate Israel because of the West."

The GA, with the theme "Together on the Frontline: One People, One Destiny," is focusing on the global challenges facing Israel and the Jewish people. Israel's Foreign Minister, Tzipi Livni, opened the GA on Sunday, while Israel's Prime Minister, Ehud Olmert, will deliver the closing keynote on Tuesday.

Joining Netanyahu to address the challenges facing the Jewish people at the outset of the 21st century was Canada's former Justice Minister and current parliament member, Irwin Cotler, who detailed a "snapshot" of threats against the Jewish community -- among them Iran.

Cotler condemned “the emergence of state-orchestrated, state-sanctioned genocidal anti-Semitism, whose epicenter has now become Ahmedinijad’s Iran, where we have the most horrific of crimes, genocide, embedded in the most virulent of hatred, anti-Semitism, impelled by avowed intent to acquire nuclear weapons.”

Cotler proposed using remedies prescribed by the Genocide Convention, including referring Ahmedinijad to the International Court of Justice for genocidal intent. 

Also on Monday, representatives of the three largest religious movements outlined the challenges and opportunities for American Jewry. Speakers included Hebrew Union College Provost Normon Cohen; Jewish Theological Seminary Chancellor-elect Arnie Eisen; and Yeshiva University President Richard Joel.

Dr. Beryl Geber, associate executive vice-president of policy development for the Jewish Federation of Greater Los Angeles, who moderated the discussion, detailed how the religious streams are working to shape tomorrow’s leaders and engage a generation “for whom MTV has always been there, Google has always been a verb … Israel has always been responsible for Gaza and the West Bank, and Jerusalem has never been divided.”

The religious leaders agreed that the next generation, while they may not be joiners, are open to meaningful experiences and connection. “Those of us who work with young people every day are not at all despondent about the Jewish future,” Eisen said. “We have to look at them as individuals with hearts and souls and minds, who can make a contribution right now.”

About addressing the challenge of reaching out to young Jews beyond the walls of insitutions like HUC, JTS, and YU, he added, “We have to give them experiences, connect them to the profundity of the Jewish past and to one another. If you meet them where they are in the journey, talk to them in their language, and embrace them with community, they respond.”

Cohen agreed. “They are a generation searching for answers. If we provide something meaningful that draws them in, they will ultimately join. They’re looking for ties and connections.” Jewish institutions of learning are “not about simply conveying knowledge and honing skills -- they need to help put together all aspects into an integrated whole that can shape a vibrant vision of Jewish life.”

But Joel added that “I worry about the attention span of our community. 'We did Jewish education, what’s next in the world?’ Jewish education doesn’t mean anything if they don’t know why they’re Jews and why it matters. The existential challenge is not raising our children, but feeding them so they will learn to understand. They have to know the story, own the story and be passionate about it.”

Also on Monday, Newsweek International editor Fareed Zakaria and French philosopher Bernard Henri-Levi, author of "Who Killed Daniel Pearl?", discussed "Emerging global realities: Terror and the challenges of radical Islam." The discussion also turned toward Iran.

"Ahmedinijad is saying things that everyone in the Arab world was saying 25 years ago about the destruction of Israel, but that are controversial today," Zakaria said. "By espousing these beliefs, he’s saying to the leaders of other world powers, 'I can say these things but you can’t, because you’re afraid of the U.S.'"

On Tuesday, Israel's Education Minister Yuli Tamir and Tourism Minister Isaac Herzog will discuss challenges facing Israel, while Bank of Israel Governor Stanley Fischer, Bank Leumi CEO and Israel GA Chair Galia Maor, media mogul Haim Saban and Israeli businessman Eitan Wertheimer will discuss investment in Israel.

Other major speakers Tuesday include business guru Warren Bennis, who will discuss "leadership at times of change."
