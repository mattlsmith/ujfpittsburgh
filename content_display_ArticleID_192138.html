A Love (and Hate) Story

By Rachel Somerstein
This story orignally appeared on JBooks.com.


Perhaps the best way to understand Savyon Liebrecht’s influence is to know that her story "Apples From the Desert" has been, for the past several years, the most frequently taught piece of fiction to students studying for matriculation exams in Israel—until now. “This year,” the Israeli writer said during a recent phone interview, “the teachers got an instruction not to teach it because the examiners got fed up with reading about it.”

Born in Germany in 1948, Liebrecht came to Israel shortly thereafter with her Holocaust-survivor parents. Though she hoped to start writing when she entered the army at 18, she did not publish her first collection of short stories until 1986. “Life came in between and I was busy doing other things—having a family and kids,” she says. “I knew I would go back to writing when the conditions allowed, and they allowed when I was 33, 34.”

At the heart of Liebrecht’s work lies an exploration of the emotional fallout of the Holocaust—a theme that colors, and at times overshadows, more earthly concerns of the flesh. For instance, the novel A Man and a Woman and a Man pivots on a lustful affair between a woman whose mother has entered a nursing facility and a man whose father has done the same. In spite of their passion—the two even discuss leaving their families to start a new life together—the heat of the lovers’ affair pales in comparison with the fierce relationship between the Holocaust-survivor mother and her daughter Hamutal. When a senile woman mistakes Hamutal for her daughter, for instance, calling her ‘mein kind,’ Hamutal’s mother practically leaps out of bed.

“‘Mein kind?’ her mother screamed, thrusting her head menacingly toward Rosa. ‘Das ist mein kind.’”

“If she had heard that ‘Das ist mein kind’ years ago,” Hamutal thinks, “everything would have been different between them.”

Ultimately, in a brief moment of lucidity, Hamutal’s mother redeems herself, telling Hamutal, “The pampered ones are the first to fall.... You had as much love as you wanted. It was pampering you didn’t get.” Their relationship shows love’s elasticity—in its pure form, nothing can make it snap. It is also a study in saying goodbye, and how much more troubling that can be within a ‘bad’ relationship as opposed to a ‘good’ one.

Whether writing fiction, plays, or for television—she coauthored her first TV script ("Dead Light") 17 years ago with friend Aliza Olmert, wife of now-Prime Minister Ehud Olmert—Liebrecht begins with a character. “Very often,” she says, the character appears “in the most inconvenient moment. She begins to tell me a bit about herself.” Liebrecht pays close attention to her characters’ dreams, explaining that to get to know what a person dreams is to get to know the person. Then, she says, she begins to write, and here she uses the word la’alot, to alight, the story on the page, letting the characters take over.

But allowing characters to steer the story is not always easy. “I sat down thinking that the end would be happy in the sense that the lovers would find themselves together,” says Liebrecht about A Man and a Woman and a Man. “But as I went on writing I understood that they were pulling each other back to their own families, and that this period of the romance was just the way of helping them to get across this difficult time. Once the parents died, then the functions of the love affair ended. And it was quite a disappointment for me.”

Part of what may explain Liebrecht’s popularity is her ability to include in her work, in addition to the creeping vine of Holocaust trauma, terrorism in present-day Israel. Such events color the writing in A Good Place for the Night: Stories, which she says she wrote “under the shadow of the second intifada.” But unlike Ian McEwan in Saturday or Jay McInerney in The Good Life—novels organized around the attacks of 9/11—terrorism appears in Liebrecht’s work as the background hum of daily life, the way that it is, more often than not (present conflict excepted), experienced in Israel.

We get especially fine examples of this in the story “Munich,” when Liebrecht’s German-based journalist phones his wife in Israel: “She tells me the situation there is just terrible—we’re shooting missiles into Gaza; two Palestinian brothers, kindergarten age, and their young aunt were killed; shots were fired at a car on the road to Hebron; and a Jewish woman in her ninth month was killed in front of her two daughters. She doesn’t know how all this killing of women and children is going to end, Yael and her friends went to a movie in the Dizengoff Center mall and for four hours, she hasn’t been able to think about anything else, but you can’t ground a girl forever... Did I buy a new Braun blender? No? What happened, did I forget? Well maybe I’ll find one in the duty-free store.” Even for this contemporary Israeli, the son of a Holocaust survivor, inherited Holocaust-trauma colors love relationships: “Of course, I can’t tell her the truth,” he thinks. “I don’t miss her or the children; all I do is worry about them and ask them to keep away from crowded places... Germany, more than any other place in the world, transports me to a new wavelength of reality where I have no commitments to others. Here, the only thing I’m committed to is my survival.” He even dreams about having “savage and tremendous” sex with blond German women, which he recognizes “is not an act of love, but an act of revenge.”

“I think usually literature needs perspective,” Liebrecht says. “It’s not journalism but somehow I think the immediate, the reality crept in. That was really the news—it really happened.” This explains, too, why so many of her stories (in fact, all of those in A Good Place for the Night) include characters in the military, even if their military affiliation is only a bit of background information, in the detail of epaulets.

Currently Liebrecht is writing a play about the affair between Hannah Arendt, the German Jewish philosopher who at 18 fell in love with her philosophy professor, Martin Heidegger, who later joined the Nazi movement. “I remember that the first plays that I handed to theatres, and it was always said—not as a compliment—they are too literary. Which means not dramatic enough,” she says. “A play has to be dramatic. The volume has to be much louder. It took me time to climb that ladder.”

Rachel Somerstein is an arts-and-culture writer based in New York. She'll begin studying for her M.F.A. in fiction at New York University in September.
