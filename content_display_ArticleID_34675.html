Holocaust Center of Greater Pittsburgh

The Holocaust Center of Greater Pittsburgh is Western Pennsylvania's only educational resource center on the Holocaust. Opened in 1980, the center's mission is to educate teachers and students; to commemorate the lives of victims, including anyone traumatized by the events of 1933-1945; and to document those events through the collection of artifacts, videos and oral histories of survivors. The programs and exhibitions heighten public awareness, honor survivors, and keep the memory alive.

The Holocaust was the end of the world of European Jewry. Six million Jews lost their lives, including one and a half million children; millions more lost families, homes, and histories. But numbers alone do not tell the story. As this horror recedes in memory, it becomes both more difficult and more important to remember the human faces of those who survived and those who did not.

The lessons of the Holocaust are universal - to cherish every life, to fight racism and prejudice, and to be responsible for one another. Keeping these values alive is the best chance we have to keep the crimes of the Holocaust from being repeated.

The work of the Holocaust Center is divided into three areas: education, documentation, commemoration and programs.

Education

Speakers Bureau

The Speakers Bureau is made up of survivors, liberators, and educators who present information about the Holocaust to schools and groups.

Exhibitions

Exhibitions that stimulate reflection on the Holocaust are a major component of our mission. Held at the center itself or at other venues in the city, exhibitions may be historical; display art inspired by the events of the Holocaust or focus on some aspect of Jewish life today.

Educational Resources

The library contains literary and historical publications, videos, audio tapes, slides, and posters as well as computers with CD-Rom capability and Internet access.

Documentation

Oral History and Video Collection

The oral history and video collection of local survivors and liberators is a priceless asset of the Holocaust Center. The center also has a collection of artifacts, including letters, photographs, and other carefully preserved items from the camps, ghettos, and journeys of displaced people.

Commemoration

Yom Hashoa

Every spring, the Holocaust Center presents a community-wide Yom Hashoa program as an opportunity to gather and remember the suffering, dignity, courage, and resistance of the victims of Nazi atrocities. The South Hills Interfaith Ministries holds a special Yom Hashoa service that is also supported by the Holocaust Center of Greater Pittsburgh.

Kristallnacht

In the fall, the center observes the anniversary of "Kristallnacht," the first instance of large-scale organized violence against Jews in Germany on November 9 and 10, 1938.

Art Collection

The center's art collection is a special way of memorializing the victims of the Holocaust. The woodcuts, sculptures, paintings, and works in other media are created by artists and area students inspired by the events and by the survivors themselves.

Programs

Arts and Writing Competition

Each year about 2,000 students take part in a seminar on a particular aspect of the Holocaust. The winners of a competition on the year's theme are published in a booklet.

Action-Reconciliation Service for Peace

Action-Reconciliation Service for Peace is a program in which German post-high-school students volunteer at a social service project. These students work in all affected countries and Israel. The Holocaust Center of Greater Pittsburgh was the first in this country to accept one of these volunteers.

Network Organization

The Holocaust Center provides a network organization for survivors and children of survivors with a newsletter, regular get-togethers, and programs that recognize the special needs of this population.

The Holocaust Center of Greater Pittsburgh is a beneficiary of the United Jewish Federation Foundation and the UJF Community Campaign.
