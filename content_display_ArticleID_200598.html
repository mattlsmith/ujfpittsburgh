ISRAEL'S PRIME MINISTER OLMERT CLOSES L.A. GENERAL ASSEMBLY: 'MOMENT OF TRUTH' WITH IRAN HAS ARRIVED, HE SAYS

 

 

Nov. 14, 2006
 

Israel's Prime Minister Ehud Olmert said Israel, the United States and the international community must unite to prevent Iran from developing nuclear weapons, during his keynote at the closing plenary of the 2006 General Assembly of United Jewish Communities/the Federations of North America in Los Angeles on Tuesday.

"We have reached the pivotal moment of truth regarding Iran. It would be an unbearable sin to future generations to allow Iran to obtain nuclear weapons," Olmert told some 3,000 of more than 5,000 total GA participants.

Olmert, who earlier this week met with President Bush, called Bush "a true friend" of Israel and said they discussed Iran "at great length." Olmert added that Israel and the world cannot allow Iran to threaten Israel's very existence.

"We cannot tolerate, nor will we tolerate, those who challenge Israel's right to exist while actively seeking to develop the catastrophic weapons to fulfill their goals," he said. "Our integrity will remain intact only if we prevent Iran's devious goals, not if we try our best but fail. We did not choose this responsibility. But the burden is ours and we cannot, we will not, we shall not shy away from confronting this challenge."

The GA, with the theme "Together on the Frontline: One People, One Destiny," focused on an array of global challenges facing Israel and the Jewish people. Olmert's comments on Iran added to the drumbeat of warnings by Israeli leaders throughout the GA. Earlier, Israeli Foreign Minister Tzipi Livni and Likud Party leader Benjamin Netanyahu said the world community must confront Iran’s nuclear ambitions, and its stated desire to destroy Israel.

Also on Tuesday, Israel's Education Minister, Yuli Tamir, and Tourism Minister, Isaac Herzog, discussed challenges facing Israel. Herzog said Israel can only confront the "axis of evil ranging from Tehran to Damascus" through a unified approach to strengthening Israel's education, economy and military, and one way to bolster Israel's economy is tourism, especially in the wake of a 40 percent drop in visitors this year because of the summer's war.

Tamir saw cause for optimism in Israel’s next generation. The $345 million Israel Emergency Campaign is “not only about recovery, but about strategic planning,” she said, with a focus on boosting literacy; improving high-school graduation rates; creating local leadership for youth and improving school infrastructure. An increasingly global economy means Israel must invest in its future resources now, she added. “Education is our future. The children of Israel have the skills and the ability to flourish.”

Bank of Israel Governor Stanley Fischer, Bank Leumi CEO and Israel GA Chair Galia Maor, media mogul Haim Saban and Israeli businessman Eitan Wertheimer also discussed investment in Israel and painted a vibrant economic picture.

The economy bounced back from the recession of the Intifida years and was strong enough to resist being dramatically affected by the war, Fischer said. “I did not invest out of Zionism; it was a pure business decision,” said Haim Saban, chair and CEO of Saban Capital Group, who purchased 30 percent of Bezeq, the telecommunications company previously owned by the governement, as well as 24 percent of Israel’s Channel 2.

Elliot Broidy, chair and CEO of the Markstone Capital Group, has acquired the Steimatzky bookstore chain and the company that owns Israel’s yellow pages. “I was motivated by the desire to get superior returns for our investors,” he said. “Strong democracy, strong accounting systems, and a solid regulatory environment and legal system were all backdrops” to his decision to invest, he said, and to Israel’s current success in the global economy.

Other major speakers Tuesday included business guru Warren Bennis, who discussed "leadership at times of change."

During the closing plenary, Olmert also congratulated outgoing UJC Board of Trustees Chair Robert Goldberg of Cleveland for his "great service" to the jewish people throughout his federation career, and welcomed incoming board Chair Joe Kanfer of Akron, Ohio, who he said will represent UJC "with honor."

UJC President and CEO Howard Rieger said the GA, which drew one of the largest number of participants in years, reflected the community's approval of a late shift in the GA program focus onto Israel.

"The attendance of 5,000 people at the GA demonstrates we did the right thing," he said.

Rieger also pointed the the power and unity of the federation system, which has raised more than $3.2 billion between the 2006 Annual Campaign, endowment funds, the Israel Emergency Campaign, the Hurrican Katrina and tsunami relief efforts, and Operation Promise to help the world's neediest Jews in the former Soviet Union and Ethiopia.

UJC was able to raise nearly $350 million for the IEC in a few months whithout "taking one dollar" for administrative costs, he added, by "working beyond business as usual."

"How's that for added value?" he said.

The UJC Board of Trustees/Delegates Assembly will meet Wednesday to discuss "translating the GA into action," by examining the driving forces of Jewish peoplehood and federation collective responsibility. Board members and delegates will also review a series of public policy resolutions.
