JDC Pesach Around the JDC World



Israel
When Hila's doctor told her that a degenerative eye condition would soon rob her of her eyesight, she was devastated. But today, thanks to the JDC-supported Peer to Peer program in Israel, she has found new strength in offering advice to others who, like herself, are newly blind or visually impaired.

Referring these individuals to doctors and other experts is just one aspect of the support she provides. "My role as a peer counselor has changed my relationship
even with my own doctor," says Hila. "Now we are colleagues who work together to help others."

Last Passover, Hila helped create a greeting card that she and her counseling peers sent to the medical professionals to whom they make referrals. The card acknowledged the counselors for the indispensable service which they provide.

"That was not the only thing it achieved," Hila adds proudly. "This card was a chance for us to say, 'Hey, look at us and the tremendous difference we are making!'"

Transplanting a Kavkazi Tradition
Hanoch fondly recalls Gov Gil, the post-Passover holiday he enjoyed in the Caucasus Mountains where he lived as a boy. On the evening following the last day of Passover, he would gather with his family and members of the Jewish Quarter in Derbent to roast a goat, sing traditional songs, eat sweet delicacies, and celebrate freedom and redemption.

"Everyone's home was open to everyone. We would go from door to door," Hanoch reminisces. "I miss those times. As a child, I used to count the days until the holiday began."

JDC helped Hanoch relive some of those memories by supporting a community Gov Gil celebration in his hometown of Akko. It was one of five such events held in Israeli cities where large numbers of Caucasus Mountain immigrants -- know as Kavkazim -- reside. The JDC festivities drew more than 3,000 participants.

Today, Gov Gil is still celebrated by thousands of Jews throughout the Caucasus Mountain region of the former Soviet Union. For many in Israel, though, the JDC programs were a first opportunity to experience the holiday and take pride in this aspect of their unique culture.

"My family never celebrated Gov Gil," says Adam, who resides in Kiryat Yam, a site of one of the JDC-supported festivities. "But it feels good to be together for a traditional Kavkazi event here in Israel."

Lithuania
5 Sedarim in Vilnius

Last Passover, the spirit of Lithuania's strong Jewish legacy was evident at five separate sedarim in the capital, Vilnius. The planners -- community activists and JDC's local community development team -- expected more than 800 guests from across the country, including both long-committed members and new faces.

One seder was held for the general community, attended by those who come only occasionally to Jewish programming, and a second seder hosted youth club children and their families. After putting together their own mini-hagada and a video that
offered a modern interpretation of the Exodus, a group of students organized a meaningful and thought-provoking seder for their membership, including Israelis studying at the university in Kaunas.

"This year I attended my first seder since my youth, and it was the first time in 30 years I'd eaten in a restaurant," cried Natasha, who celebrated the holiday at the golden age club Passover festivities. Students escorted those seniors who could not reach the event independently. After celebrating the 90th birthday of one member, participants offered up a rousing rendition of Chad Gadya, and enjoyed a merging of their past and the community's future.

But for middle generation members who grew up during the Soviet era with no memories of Jewish life, the Gesher club seder was especially meaningful. The
evening symbolized their newfound Jewish pride and their longing to strengthen their Jewish identity and knowledge. In fact, for most, last year's seder was their first. They spent the weeks before the holiday studying its significance, songs, and traditions, and preparing to participate in the seder itself.

"You can't imagine how important it is for me to attend a seder and to make the berachot," said Simon. "I'm sure if my parents were alive, they would cry
from joy."

Croation, Slovenia & Poland
Seder by the Sea

In Split, a small coastal town along Croatia's Adriatic Sea, the community expected only 35 members to participate in the Passover seder, but optimistically set the table for 40. As the guests arrived, the women preparing the meal looked at each other in amazement: more than 50 people had gathered to celebrate Pesach! In the holiday spirit, they all managed to sit comfortably and the food was plentiful -- including
the seder plate greens and huevos inhaminados, the red eggs that symbolize Sephardic tradition in the former Yugoslavia.

Everyone joined in the seder, singing along and celebrating the miracle of the Exodus. They acknowledged two modern-day milestones, as well. A member of their own community -- Luciano, a JDC-sponsored yeshiva student -- had come home from Israel to lead the festivities, and Lovre, 9, the grandson of a community
member, was visiting from London and arrived just in time to ask the "Ma Nishtana" and hide the afikoman.

"It has been almost ten years since we had our first community seder here -- shortly after the break-up of Yugoslavia and the beginning of the wars," remembers
Ana, the community activist who organized the evening. "It is indeed a miracle to see how far we have come."

A Dynamic Journey
Slovenian Chief Rabbi Ariel Haddad fondly recalls the community's first traditional Passover seder, held in 1999 in the community's small meeting room in the nation's capital, Ljubljana. The rabbi brought kosher food from his home in nearby Trieste, Italy, and the handful of members in attendance used a photocopied hagadah. "Slovenian Jewry has since come a long way," the rabbi announced proudly.

So much so that the seder now takes place in the dining hall of the city's finest hotel, which donates the space for the nearly 100 participants and gives the rabbi sole use of the kitchen so that kosher food can be prepared on site. The highlight, though, is the new Hebrew-Slovenian hagadah -- the first Hebrew text ever printed in Slovenia -- published with help from JDC and the Slovenian government. Like its more famous cousin, the Sarajevo Hagadah, the Slovenian edition is an important symbol of its community, exemplified by a special version of "Dayenu" which traces the community's history since the republic's independence in 1991.

"There is still a long way to go," said Rabbi Haddad, addressing the crowd at this past year's seder. "But we all look forward to making the dynamic journey together."

A Seder of Their Own
In preparation for Passover, JDC sponsored three Polish Jewish activists to attend the Hillel Seder Pesach Seminar in Kiev, where they learned the practicalities of leading a seder and how to make the evening a meaningful experience for participants of all ages and backgrounds. There the activists also reclaimed a piece of their Jewish heritage that will ultimately provide the foundation for new Polish
Jewish community traditions.

Tomasz, one of the Kiev seminar participants, returned to Warsaw where he conducted the seder on both nights of Passover. "It was the first time I'd ever led a seder, and it was the most important event of my life," he recalls emotionally.

For Bartek, too, leading the seder in Bielsko-Biala was monumental, both personally and for the community; it was the first time someone under the age of 30 had done so in as long as anyone there could remember. Together with the JDC Jewish Service Corps volunteer, Bartek conducted the second seder in Czestochowa, an evening that proved particularly poignant. Not only was it the community's first seder in many years, but a young man who had recently celebrated his bar
mitzvah -- another community milestone -- asked the four questions.

"Passover unites us," said Bartek, referring to the JDC-supported holiday festivities. "Celebrating together helps us come to terms with the past, and gives us hope for the future."

A Village Seder
Lying across the Arabian Sea from the Gateway of India, two hours south by catamaran to the Mandra jetty and then by bus to the interior of the Konkan region, is the small village of Revdanda. Many Jews here and in the surrounding villages live
below the poverty line, so celebrating Passover at home is way beyond their means.

Instead, they don their finest attire and come to a JDC-sponsored seder at Revdanda's Beth El Synagogue. JDC staff and volunteers greet eager participants, who arrive ready to sing holiday songs and to participate in discussions on the hagadah in Marathi, the language most commonly spoken in the community.
Participants also join in a special seder meal, consisting of traditional Bene Israel Passover fare.

"JDC's special efforts help us to understand the importance of the festival more clearly," says David, the synagogue president. "This is one of the reasons why Judaism is still alive in our region."
