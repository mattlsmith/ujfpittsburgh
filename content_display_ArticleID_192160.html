"The Partnership Report"- An On The Scene Bulletin From Karmiel & Misgav

Aug. 13, 2006


To our dear friends and partners,

Today is Sunday, the 33rd day of the war.
Damage afflicted to a house on Hadas St. in Karmiel this past Thursday Aug. 8th. This is the 3rd time the street was attacked.
In spite of all the talk of a "cease fire" and maybe because of it, quiet has yet to fall upon our region. In fact, quite the opposite is true even as I write. The weekend was not calm and today as well started with sirens wailing throughout the morning hours, going on throughout the day. We "enjoyed" most of today in shelters or secure rooms.

Last night our forces fighting in Lebanon continued to absorb severe losses and at this hour as well, harsh battles are being fought in Lebanon and we still unaware of the full cost we will be forced to pay.

Damage caused to a Karmiel home on Rotem St. during the Shabbat as a result of a Katyusha rocket attack in the front yard.
Throughout the weekend, a number of rocket barrages fell in the region, primarily in open areas. One Katyusha did however fall in the yard of a Karmiel home causing property damage to a number of adjacent homes and a number of victims suffering from anxiety who were treated and released.

  
Today rounds also fell in Karmiel. Fortunately, all of them landed in non-populated areas.

It is expected that at least until the "cease-fire" enters into effect, the north will be heavily shelled. What comes after that, no one knows. We can only wait?

From Left: Leviah Fisher Shalev- Karmiel Press Spokeswoman, Maj. General Dan Ronen, Northern District Police Commander and Adi Eldar, Mayor of Karmiel
The Home Front Command and other security agencies have instructed residents that although a cease-fire is expected to take effect tomorrow morning at 8:00 AM (Israel time), there is no change in the security instructions concerning the region. Therefore, residents are still required to stay in their homes close to shelters or secure rooms.

Dan Ronen, Northern District Commander of the Israel Police met with Karmiel's mayor Adi Eldar on a regular basis to discuss with him the situation in the north. This past Friday the two met in the city's command center.

Boaz Shviger (center) and his staff from the Souther Sharon Regional Council during their visit to Karmiel
Boaz Shviger, Press Spokesman for the Southern Sharon Regional Council organized a contribution drive among council employees for Karmiel and the north. This past Friday Shoiger and his staff arrived in Karmiel with 120 fans purchased for Karmiel's shelters.

Throughout this war, I have offered you from time to time a glimpse at the ways local residents have been able to cope with the situation. Today I wish to present you with another report, the third in a series written by Shuki Levinger, the managing director of "Kishorit", a Misgav residential community for adults with special needs.

Dear Friends,
It is 6:00 a.m. Saturday morning and I’ve come up from the shelter to take advantage of the quiet and send you a few lines.

In New York they may have agreed on a resolution but there is no evidence of that here on the ground, no feeling that we are winding anything down. There is no clarity about anything, and that is the source of deepest fear.

On a personal note, I evacuated my own family. The younger ones became too terrified to stay. Having them here in Kishorit gave me enormous comfort but they are too young for this ordeal. They headed south, and I hope to have them back here soon.
 
But the real pain I feel is for our members. Some are close to falling apart – and will doubtless do just that as soon as they feel it is safe enough. As you know, if there are any people for whom routine, familiarity and order are essential to wellbeing, it is our community and now almost everything we have managed to create for them at Kishorit has suddenly changed, and the homes they established here have abruptly become unsafe. The security and solitude of their own apartments which provide such important emotional protection have been replaced by rocket-dodging in frighteningly small, crowded underground spaces. Little wonder that fragile people are on the brink of collapse.

We shut down the dining room, which produced the moment of greatest mass misery so far: tears of anger, then howls of frustration and incomprehension. Who among them actually understands to what extent our lives are in danger? I don’t really know. But congregating in the dining room three times a day was judged too dangerous, and I tried to explain. Like regular soldiers on the front line, we get Kishorit rations now. A hot meal is such a natural source of comfort, something to look forward to, something that makes you feel warm inside and nurtured. That, too, has been taken away.

And there is the staff, most with families of their own, all here and close to exhaustion. The alarm goes off and someone must do the rounds to bring everyone quickly in. To run from the clubhouse to the residential area takes two minutes; the rocket on its way from Tyre to Kishorit gets here in only one. So who will be sent to look for members: an older grandfather, a young father with small children at home, or someone single who hasn’t yet seen much of life?

Some of my decisions may have considerable financial implications on the future of Kishorit. Kishorit is not a business, and we have always been very careful indeed about money; now I find myself having to authorize the outlay of the kind of money Kishorit does not have on protective measures. It is that impossible balance between fiscal responsibility and keeping people safe.

I suspect that already I’ve made more decisions during this war than in my entire life put together, or at least it seems that way; decisions that have to do with life, death and trying to guess what the future will hold.

As soon as I can, I shall write again. This is just to keep you informed.
Yours,           
Shuki

The "personal angle" I've chosen to conclude today's report is "war journal" that describes that experiences and events as viewed by a resident of one of our Misgav communities. Although written "way back then" on August 8, its words remain current.


My Diary/ A Woman in Misgav

On Sunday morning, I saw my cousin's email that said that he had waited throughout the Shabbat to receive my mail summing up the days and events of my week. It was then that I understood that even this seemingly simple act, to sit down and write, demands an energy level that I simply don't have. Moreover, how can I summarize anything when the news changes once every 5 minutes? How could I have reflected on what's happened when every morning began with a tense quiet and towards the afternoon, "all hell broke loose" with the pounding of Katyusha rockets- some of them in our vicinity, others throughout the Galilee and the smell of fire permeated the air all around us? What was I to write when your heart sinks and the mood sours daily, by the hour, by the moment as you look for names you know among the fallen?

Nonetheless, today is Tuesday August 8, 2006 and I am writing the following sentences.

A week, it seems like eternity.

One of my son's who was called up for reserve duty was home for a few days. He seems more mature, more responsible. He's faced war. He's stopped shaving, tired. The shooting is endless in Lebanon. He says that they are doing a good job and are very much appreciated for their efforts. It seems to me that he knows his job well, is able to live with it and even feels some pride in what he's done.

My second son just showed up for a brief 24-hour break. Like a lion in a cage, he wanders back and forth, unable to find himself. Constantly on the phone, preparing a variety of lists that he needs to continue his job "inside". Even the smallest word or sign of affection angers him. He maintains self-control and doesn't let himself get carried away. While it is clear that physically he is at home; mentally and emotionally he's still there. We took of his shoes and put his big "military" feet in a soothing bucket of sanitizing and calming bath ointments. Maybe through his feet we will be able to calm his soul.

My daughter, the student has "shut down" her dorm room in Haifa until the new school year begins, and returned home. It's amazing to see how many possessions a young girl can collect in just a few years out of the house. Nonetheless, my orderly and well-calculated daughter managed to re-organize herself in her room, organizing and arranging EVERYTHING! Since last Thursday, she has been coordinating the regional council day camp in our community's kindergarten. A large part of the time, they are in the shelter. Together with a girl friend, they are organizing a variety of activities to keep the kids busy and allow them to vent their excess energies. Of course playing outside in the yard is impossible, it's forbidden. A variety of volunteers came to the small shelter to entertain the children: storytellers, petting farms and physical activities, etc. One of the banks contributed a fan.

My husband is very busy at work. He's got a lot of responsibility. New projects need to be developed in zero time. He's tired, worried. The silent type, doesn't say much, supportive, always has a good word to say, comforting and encouraging. Quietly listens to my endless babbling.

My friend who lives in the center of the country, called again. She's worried. She's dying to host a "family from the north". She wants to do her part in the national effort. She wants to help and spoil someone in her own way.

On Friday morning, we headed south to my sister in-law in Ashkelon. She, in her quiet, "matter of fact" and most effective manner stood before us, silently embracing and allowing us our space, spoiling us with her delicious homemade foods. That evening a couple and their two small children from Ashdod joined us. They brought with them the sanity of a normal family who lives in a normal region, a normal way of life. Around the dinner table, there was the hustle and bustle of a family totally dedicated to raising and educating their children. I, on the other hand can't find a place for myself.

Saturday morning, we headed home. On the way north, we stopped to visit friends who live in Yavneh. Their son, a fighter in a paratroop reconnaissance unit was home with them. He had been slightly wounded. The relief and acceptance of the situation are etched in his parent's faces. From their standpoint, their personal travail has ended. Their son is home. My friend runs around serving, toiling and spoiling. Beverages and fruit on the table; lunch is in the oven. At the same time, she radiates a sense of relaxation, of calm. My friend, my soul mate doesn't pressure me, gently offers, dedicates herself to me, enters my soul. However, I- drag with me the restlessness that has affected me here as well. I have a burning desire to return home, to return to the illogical security of my own four walls. Today is Tuesday. Since last Wednesday, we've had a daily rate of nearly 200 missiles hit in and around the Galilee region. Our community's security coordinator said yesterday dozens of rockets struck the hills above our home.

Between the rocket attacks, we've all tried to create a new reality, one that holds within it a small, a very small portion of our old reality. In this new reality, one shops in Karmiel only when it is absolutely necessary. Even then, I got caught in a rocket attack, forcing me into the store's shelter. We were two cashiers, a few employees and about three customers and me. We quietly and thankfully waited the obligatory 15 minutes. A rocket fell in the back behind the shopping center. Its explosion was strongly felt and heard. The primary objectives of this new reality are to survive the journey from home to work and back again, to listen for incoming rounds, to determine their proximity and decide accordingly whether it is prudent to head for a "secure room".

On my way home, I see the pillars of smoke rising from yesterday's attack. The rockets fell near the city's cemetery. The smoke, from the city's old garbage dump, is carried along by the wind. No one bothers to extinguish the fires.

Once more I feel a sense of exhaustion, I'm tired of all this. I pray for some quiet, for something and someone sane, who will bring order out of all this chaos. I pray for my children to all come home so that once again we can see them with smiles on their faces.

However, do not draw the wrong conclusion out of all that's written above. We're fine. We are strong both physically and emotionally, all of us together and as individuals. We've become a bit introspective trying to digest the new experiences forced upon us that demand tremendous stores of energy. Nonetheless, we go on, strengthening and supporting one another as best we can.

That's it for today, Tuesday August 8, 2006 3:00PM.

That's all for today, let us pray that quiet will be restored and peace and security will return to the region and the country.

Sincerely yours,
Yael Raich
