"The Partnership Report"- An On The Scene Bulletin From Karmiel & Misgav

July 24, 2006


To our dear friends and partners,

Today is Monday and the 13th day of the war.

Last night, thankfully passed quietly and finally we had the chance to get a full night's sleep. The next day began quiet enough, allowing all of us to get some fresh air and do urgent errands.

The Aftermath of a Rocket Attack in Karmiel
Now at 4:00 in the afternoon, as I am writing the daily update, sirens began to wail followed by the sounds of explosions.

All of us had hoped that we would get through the day in peace. Unfortunately, it wasn't to be. Moreover, following the sirens I just wrote about, another home in Karmiel received a direct hit. Luckily, the Katyusha rocket exploded after hitting the ground, causing "only" relatively minor damage to the house.
                                                                  
The flow of VIPs continues with visits from Eli Ishai, the minister of Trade, Industry and Labor and Benjamin (Fuad) Ben Eliezer, the minister of National Infrastructures.

Today I decided to give the stage to Shula Menachem and Orna Schweitzer, the directors of Welfare Services for Karmiel and Misgav who described their departments' respective tasks during these difficult days and how they were set up to cope with the current state of emergency in the region.
  
Karmiel

Fuad and Karmiel Crisis Management Team in the City's Command Post
As director of Karmiel's Social Services Administration, Shula is a member of the Crisis Management Team (CMT) responsible for the city's operation, as of yesterday from within its underground command post. 

The municipality established a Crisis Response Team (CRT) composed of social workers from the welfare services department, psychologists, volunteers from the city's Volunteer Unit and psychiatrists or psychiatric social workers. Like the Crisis Management Team of senior municipal leadership, they too operate from within an underground bomb shelter 24/7 to respond to a wide range of individual and family emergencies. The staff maintains regular telephone contact with needy individuals and conducts visits to numerous homes, bomb shelters and area hospitals to visit the city's injured residents. Contact is also maintained with the city's senior citizens, handicapped individuals, single-parent families and new immigrants and others who require their services because of the current situation including the parents and families of those soldiers who have fallen in this most recent warfare. Every day the team meets to map the city's needs and plan appropriate responses. 

The city's residents are in a situation of fear and "survival", concentrating primarily on their physical needs.  Therefore, a large portion of the team's assistance is devoted to this area, e.g. food for infants and the elderly, disposable diapers and medication, etc. The team also deals in caring for the resident's emotional needs and "matching" between Karmiel residents who temporarily wish to leave the region and those families from the south and central parts of the country willing to host them in their homes.

As hostilities began, the department led a mapping tour of all of the city's public shelters and the city is making every effort to supply the shelters with fans, emergency lighting, radios, games, toys and food according to each shelter's specific needs.  Moreover, private individuals have also contributed equipment that is significant and critical for facilitating the residents' "forced" existence in the shelters. A tour of the shelters is conducted on a daily basis by city officials and volunteers to meet residents' immediate needs.

Misgav 
In her role as director of the region's Welfare Services Department Orna is also a member of the region's Crisis Management Team (CMT) that is responsible for providing services to the region's 35 communities.

City Employees Participating in a Laughter Workshop to Relieve Stress
Like Karmiel, the Misgav team operates from within a command post manned by regional officials and is responsible for daily operation and the region's immediate response in case of an emergency. The team meets twice a day for an update on the situation in the region and for determining methods of intervention.

The Misgav Crisis Response Team includes social workers, psychologists and the director of the Community Center as well as the directors of the Education and Volunteer Services Departments.

The team operates on two levels:
1. Assistance initiated and provided by the Response Team to communities and families at risk
2. The establishment and operation of a telephone information center and internet forum (under the auspices of the Regional Psychological Services) that provides answers, instruction and guidance to the communities' residents
 
In order to reach council residents small "field teams" have been dispersed to meet with community officials, families in need and to area hospitals to visit with those residents of the region who have been injured in the current warfare.

The mayor and senior CMT members who visit the communities on a regular basis found that the residents' primary concern was a lack of organized activities for the region's children. This brought about a decision to mobilize the Community Center staff, led by the center's director Liat Belcar to conduct a series of community-based day camps in local bomb shelters and other protected structures.

In the Bedouin Sector-
Because of its different communal structure (a lack of significant public leadership), the regional council provides the sector with community-wide instructions, means and methods for coping with the situation, emotional support, answers and written information in Arabic.

As in Karmiel, the Misgav CRT conducted a mapping tour of the region's shelters and worked with the communities to equip and adapt the shelters in accordance to their specific needs.

We have no doubt that the two welfare teams working around the clock are doing a fantastic and blessed job! All we can do now is hope for the best and pray together for an end to this war. 

Today, in the framework of the "Personal Diary" written by local area residents, I have selected "My War", written by Teddy Sable, a Karmiel resident and local journalist.

"My War"- Teddy Sable
"It all began sometime around 3PM on Thursday July 13, 2006. I was sitting in our offices in Karmiel's industrial area speaking on the phone with our graphic artist, just another consultation that today seems so unimportant, when a round of Katyusha rockets landed across the road in Dir El Assad. Our graphic artist, she lives nearby in Gilon, heard it as well. Shocked and frightened, we slammed down the receivers and I rushed to Noam's kindergarten. I drove like no less than a laser guided missile was after me. Air raid sirens in Karmiel, what's going on here?!  Air raid sirens in Karmiel, is this possible?!

By the time I reached the kindergarten, the kids were already in the shelter. Noam couldn't have been happier. Amos (their male kindergarten teacher) was already "preaching" why they were in the shelter, what war means and of course, we will be victorious. We sat in the shelter for an hour after which we returned home. That evening and the night-time passed quietly. It was also quiet on Friday, the next day. That is to say, there was no shelling. Maybe it's over? That evening I said to Zohar (my significant other) that I don't care if the world stands on its head, I was going out for my daily jogging. I made it back in peace. No way can those missiles reach us. No way! To Karmiel?!

They came the next morning. It was around 10:00AM when a round of rockets landed on the Givat Ram neighborhood of Karmiel. It seems that the world indeed was going to stand on its head. We looked at each other and both immediately understood that they were approaching us. We don't have a "secure room" in our apartment. Zohar said that that was OK because the building was constructed from reinforced concrete. "Don't you remember how long it took for me to open a window in the dining room wall? There were two of us with a pneumatic jackhammer and a 10-pound hammer banging away for two days!" Of course, I remember. How could I forget?

"Don't forget that we live on the ground floor and any missile has to pass through the neighbor's roof on the second floor", he added. Now I am calm. There is no way that a missile with succeed in breaking through the walls of our house. Nevertheless, I went to check on the neighborhood shelter. The situation wasn't good. There's no electricity, no water and it was simply filthy. For a moment, I thought of enlisting the neighbors for a clean-up operation, but that thought passed quickly enough. What, me clean?! 

I returned home. Noam was instructed not to leave the house and that he could only play inside. Zohar could only leave when he had to have a smoke. We agreed that if there was an attack, everyone should run to the guestroom. Zohar claimed that it was the safest. I reminded him that it had a giant bay window facing the garden. "It doesn't matter since the window faces the south and the missiles are coming from either the east or west. "I understood that there was no chance that the missile could enter the window. "And what about shrapnel" I asked. "It flies high, the window is relatively low, so it won't come through the window". I calmed me.

It was exactly 1:00PM. The round of rockets screamed through the air. The noise was deafening, the walls of the house shook. Instinctively I ran to Noam, grabbed his hand and dragged him into the guestroom. I shoved him to the floor, hugging him to protect him with my body. Another 30 seconds and another horrendous boom shook the house. Zohar was on the floor beside me. We were both shaking. So was Noam. Silent, we hugged the interior wall. Then another strong boom, that one was close. As if, it had fallen in our garden. We were scared that the next one would fall on us. We all covered our heads with our hands. Then there was silence, deafening silence. This was followed by the harsh smell of gunpowder in the air, the smell of death.

The silence continued. We got up from the floor, peeked out the window. No, it hadn't fallen in our garden. Zohar and I looked at one another and without a word, agreed that it was time to get out of here. Fast. Immediately. Fear and hopelessness- had beaten us.

Refugees
We're in Holon. For the past week, we've been staying with relatives. It's hot. We miss our cats and the house. We miss our/my regular habits, the small things in life that I've adopted and glorified throughout the years, the daily routine that makes my day, my life so well organized, so sane. Forget it, I told myself. Now you are in someone else's home. Get use to being a guest. Ask before you change a channel, open the refrigerator, ask for a bath towel and learn where the toilet paper is stored when it runs out. There are at least 100,000 people in this country having the same experience.

We started looking for a solution for next week. After a few phone calls to the "adoption information center", a wonderful family from Moshav Mishmaret volunteered to host us. I began to wonder as to what level of kindness or nobility is required from a family to accept from one minute to the next, a family of total strangers. People who they had never met before and all odds said that they would never hear from them again, after they left.

So far, so good. We've been here for the past 24 hours. The first night I cried terribly and told Zohar that I wanted to go home. He tried to calm me down. "Teddy, maybe we should wait a day or two. Let's see what happens in Karmiel. Do you prefer having a rocket land on your head?" No, that was not an option.

Our hosts are lovely people. They've really gone out of their way to make us feel comfortable. They too love animals and raise a horse, a dog, parrots, hens, ducks and a jackass named Amnon. Noam is in seventh Heaven. As far as he's concerned, the war could continue?"

That's all for today, let us pray that quiet will be restored and peace and security will return to the region and the country.

Sincerely yours,
Yael Raich
