"The Partnership Report"- An On The Scene Bulletin From Karmiel & Misgav

July 26, 2006


To our dear friends and partners,

Today is Wednesday, the 15th day of the war and a difficult day for both our forces operating in Lebanon and the cities and communities throughout the north still suffering from indiscriminate shelling and Katyusha rockets.

The Aftermath of a Katyusha Attack in Karmiel
From the early morning hours until the hour in which I am writing this report there have been four rounds of Katyusha rockets, numerous warnings (sirens) instructing us to enter our secure areas/shelters and over 15 rockets that struck the Karmiel-Misgav region, one directly hitting a home in Karmiel. Fortunately, the house was unoccupied at the time of the attack.

In both municipalities the routine of a "state of emergency" continues- daily situation updates, tours in the field, visits to the various shelters and locations hit by the missiles, more visits to the elderly and the handicapped and once more answering the needs and requests of the region's residents.

The installation of air-conditioners in public shelters continued again today. The distribution of toys and games (contributed) to the children in the shelters was completed today.

Hagai Merom, treasurer of the Jewish Agency and senior officials from the "Amigur" Housing Co., (an agency owned subsidiary responsible for  the construction and maintenance of public housing and rent control apartments in Karmiel and throughout Israel) visited Karmiel today. During their visit, they expressed their admiration and appreciation to Adi Eldar and the city's employees for the way in which they've continued to manage the city in light of the situation.

The mayor responded by telling his guests "in spite of the difficulties facing the residents, their spirit is steadfast and they are prepared to make an additional effort as long as there is the understanding that the end of this war will bring about a permanent solution for the country and the area." In response to their questions concerning the needs and difficulties facing the city the mayor noted that the city still lacks 25 air-conditioning units for shelters located in a number of apartment buildings throughout the city and at the end of this week, funding will end for the hot meals provided to the city's elderly population.

Karmiel's Police and Mayor Enjoy a Pita and Labne Break
Between Katyusha rockets, sirens and tours to check the damage an unusual visit occurred today when Chief Superintendent Majad Amar, deputy commander of Karmiel's police station was surprised to see his wife and mother at the entrance of the stationhouse, with stacks of pita bread and "labne" (a smooth white cheese spread, usually prepared from goat's milk) for his policemen. All this was happening just as the mayor was arriving for an inspection of the station. After enjoying the delicious break in his hectic day, the mayor thanked the officer's wife and mother for their initiative and told them "this is the best pita and labne I've ever tasted!"

Organized activities for children and young people in shelters and protected areas are continuing, as well as activities outside the region, in the center of the country. Thanks to a collaborative effort between the Jewish Agency and the HaNoar HaOved V'HaLomed Youth Movement, 160 Karmiel children enjoyed some moments of "joy and relaxation" at a summer camp at the "Kfar Batya" Youth Village and 130 7th and 8th graders from the Misgav region enjoyed 3 days of fun and hospitality in Holon. The group that left today joined the 80 teenagers who departed the day before for a three-day respite at the "Neve Erezim" Community Center in Holon, coordinated by the Israel Community Centers Co. There they were welcomed by a group of 50 Holon teenagers who will host them for meals and showers at their homes, accompany them throughout their stay and participate with them in a portion of their activities. Another group of 100 teenagers who were guests at the Ramat Eliahu Community Center in Rishon Le'Zion will return home to the region today.

Children who stayed home were awarded today with "moments of joy" when a troupe of well-known actors   from the Cameri Repertory Theatre performed for them in city shelters.

The war and forced home confinement has seriously disrupted the region's economy and residents' ability to earn a livelihood, particularly independent businesses and artists. The City of Tel Aviv graciously responding to a request from local artists whose income has been disrupted because of the ongoing warfare invited them to participate at no charge in two upcoming Tel Aviv Arts Fairs. Eight Misgav artists will participate in two fairs scheduled to take place this weekend in Tel Aviv.

Today as well, I want to bring you a more personal angle of how our area's residents are coping with this difficult situation. Today I've selected portions from a journal written by Malka Maor, a resident of the Misgav community of Gilon. Malka, a journalist and newspaper editor is known to many Pittsburghers as the head of the Israel Memorial Day and Independence Day Youth Delegation for the past two years, a program sponsored by Partnership 2000 and the Jewish Agency. 

To My Dear Friends, Shalom,
Between one Katyusha to the next, between running to and from the "secure room", often many times during the day (thankfully, Nasarallah lets us sleep at night), an ever-growing list of unusual, yet enriching experiences has been added to my store of personal life experiences. I've decided to share some of them with you, albeit not including a chronological report of the events as they've occurred in our region (this is being more than adequately done by other good and dedicated friends from the Karmiel and Misgav region). 

The "Secure Zone"
Let's begin with the ritual familial gatherings in the "Secure Zone" (BTW, I think that this war/operation should be given the codename "Secure Zone"- because the IDF is no doubt fighting this war to provide us with a "secure zone" throughout the north and is ordering us into our own private or public secure zones throughout the hostilities?).

As a mother of adult children and teenagers, I must take this opportunity to thank Nasarallah; thanks to him, we've had some lengthy and quite lovely "quality time" together over the past ten days. My son and his girlfriend, who during "normal" times live on Kibbutz Dan, near the "Tel Hai Regional College" (near Kiryat Shmona) where they are both students, came to stay with us immediately following the beginning of events in the north, and joined our two daughters already at home. Since then, we have found ourselves spending hours together, talking, playing cards and other games. In other words, here at our house the morale is high!

A few days ago, because of the continuation of current military operations/war we were however forced to make a number of strategic decisions that while disturbing our idyllic state, showed our concern for a number of the individuals within the family. Our students, for example relocated to the country's central region because of a decision to conduct all of Tel Hai's final examinations in Tel Aviv. The couple found a warm and welcome home with friends from where they were able to commute daily to the Tel Aviv University campus where students from the north had been granted free use of the school's academic facilities, so that they would be able to study for their upcoming finals and of course receive passing grades.

Our younger daughter, age16½, organized a training camp for her basketball team ("Hapoel Emek Yizrael"-National League) in Ra'anana until the end of the week. The players were hosted by team members of "Hapoel Ra'anana. At the beginning of next week she'll fly to Holland with two girl friends to stay for two weeks with my son and his wife who live in Amsterdam (he studies there), who cancelled their vacation in Israel (clearly understood?) and instead volunteered to accept three refugees from the bombed out north who needed to "clear their heads" for awhile.

We stayed behind in order to get exercise- running between the "secure room" on the second floor and the "secure room" on the ground floor of the house!

Helloooooo- Is This the Same Country? The Experiences of A Northerner In The Central Part Of The Country
Yesterday (Monday July 24) I witnessed history being made in the State of Israel, i.e. the development of a new folklore in the protected home front ("knock on wood"), i.e. the home front from Hadera northward. Because of the family decision to send my daughter to Holland for two weeks of R & R, we quickly checked to see that all her documents were in order. To our surprise, we discovered that she had only two more weeks on her passport and that we had to renew it immediately since she was scheduled to fly to Europe in another six day. Because of the shelling, the offices of the Ministry of Interior Affairs were closed in Karmiel, Akko, the Bay Area and Haifa. So what were we to do? Why travel to the nearest open office, of course, which just happened to be in the lovely city of Hadera. At the same time, it was clear that since we had abandoned the north, we were going to call a "timeout" from the war, visit relatives in Petah Tikva and do some shopping in a big city shopping center. Because as I said, my daughter who is traveling to Holland is missing some clothes, which are of course extremely critical for her trip (for some reason we women always seem to discover that just before a trip abroad, suddenly we have absolutely nothing to wear?). Of course, my other daughter and I decided that we too couldn't pass up an opportunity like this either, who knows when the shops in the north will reopen?

We arrived in Hadera. What can I say? I rubbed my eyes in amazement. There were restaurants open, cars on the road, people strolling down the street with small children in hand, in strollers, enjoying ice cream cones. It took me back; back to the days long ago, when one could see things like that where we lived. We crossed the busy town square, and arrived at the shopping center (open!) where the offices of the Ministry of Interior Affairs were located. Hundreds of people sat there (and not in "secure zones") waiting their turn to receive a passport\ID card or some other document(s) they needed from the ministry in Hadera. We took a number (678!). In a moment of resourcefulness (it happens sometimes), I decided to go to the "fast lane". I presented myself to the clerk as a resident of the north in urgent need of a passport renewal for my daughter. Amazingly, the woman with a giant smile across her face provided us with prompt, efficient and immediate service and even informed me that the new passport would be ready within 30 minutes and we could receive here, on the spot! It was simply amazing (especially when you consider that it usually takes a few days in the mail, but now there is no mail service?)!

We sat out the 30-minute wait in the coffee shop next store. After ordering coffee, the waiter asked with a smile, "you're from the north, right?" Surprised I answered, "yes, how did you know?" He responded, "it shows you're not from here, you look different?" What a shocking discovery! It is possible identify us, the northerners!

Happy and in good spirits, with a new passport in hand, we made our way south, to Petah Tikva. The usually irritating traffic jams of the center of the country didn't bother us. We were about to go shopping after a two week dry spell. Yeah!

There were more surprises and experiences in store for us during our shopping in the large and crowded shopping center in Petah Tikva. First, the sales people all recognized us as being from the north (how? I always thought that I looked normal, and if not me, then certainly my two princesses?). Many stores had giant signs in the windows announcing special discounts for residents of the north! All that was left to do was show them an ID card and the discount (between 5-50%) was yours. Sounds simple, doesn't it? Well it wasn't. After I presented them with my ID card that had me registered as a resident of Gilon, the questions began. "Where's Gilon? What's Gilon?" they asked. "It's in Misgav." I said. "Where's that?" they asked. "Near Karmiel", I told them. "Ah, I've heard of Karmiel, Katyusha rockets are falling all around there, prove to me that you're from there!" My telephone number, which is certainly a northern number, was sufficient to convince them that there is a community called Gilon that the number of residents living there is about the same of a high-rise apartment building in Petah Tikva and that its residents are spending most of their time these days in "secure zones"?

Then there was this: while my daughter and I were waiting in line to try on a pair of jeans in one of the stores, "I was forced" to listen to a conversation between two women who stood by me. One said she was from a community belonging to Merom Hagalil Regional Council (north of Misgav, along the border and also in the line of fire) and the second told us she was from Kiryat Motzkin (in the Haifa Bay region and also targeted), i.e. both refugees from the bombarded northern regions, now visiting in the center of the country. The woman from Merom Hagalil told us how the regional council had taken care of everything including home hospitality, day camps and activities for the kids (therefore, she was able to go shopping), while the woman from Kiryat Motzkin complained that she had to arrange everything on her own, including of course, shopping in the shopping center.

I won't continue to bore you with all that happened in the shopping center.  To make a long story short; we left with bags full, with my youngest daughter declaring that from now on she would only shop in the center of the country as there was no way that the stores in the north would ever have anywhere the same variety of clothing!

I declared, "your father is really going to have a score to settle with Nasarallah, not only has he made it impossible to work in the region, there will be no income, but the war also cost him an expensive shopping trip and a flight ticket to Holland?"!

So with a true smile and high morale, I say goodbye, for now.

Sincerely yours,
Malka

That's all for today, let us pray that quiet will be restored and peace and security will return to the region and the country.

Sincerely yours,
Yael Raich
