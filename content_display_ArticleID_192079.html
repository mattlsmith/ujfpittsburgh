Hospital treats Hezbollah fighters in emergency unit but refuses to let them stay

By KATHY GANNON (Associated Press Writer)
Associated Press
08/12/2006


TYRE, Lebanon - The biggest hospital in the warzone of south Lebanon has treated a few wounded Hezbollah guerrillas in its emergency unit - but never admits them to stay, fearing their presence would make the facility a target for Israeli attack, its chairman says.

Jabal Amal Hospital, the largest medical facility in the port city of Tyre, is where most of the victims of Israel's offensive in the south have been treated - almost all of them civilians caught in Israeli bombardment and airstrikes.

The wave of wounded has overwhelmed the 173-bed hospital, where the usual staff of 102 doctors has dropped to only 38 because of staffers fleeing the fighting.

The remaining doctors, along with 109 nurses and other staffers and 50 refugees, have moved into the hospital because moving back and forth to their homes each day has become too dangerous amid Israeli bombardment.

Since the outbreak of hostilities a month ago, Jabal Amal has treated 554 civilian war wounded, Dr. Ahmad Mroue told The Associated Press. In addition, between 20 to 25 wounded Hezbollah fighters have been treated in the emergency unit.

"We told them (Hezbollah leaders) that we will do all the medical treatment that is necessary in the emergency unit, but we can't keep them in the hospital. We also do operations for them and then they take them the same day," Mroue, the private hospital's chairman, said Friday, wearing his green scrubs.

Only one guerrilla has died at the hospital, and his body was sent to Tyre's smaller, government-run hospital where bodies have been stored because it has a large refrigeration truck.

Guerrillas are distinguishable by khaki uniforms they wear, and those that have come through Jabal Amal have mainly been seriously wounded with gunshot injuries, Mroue said.

It appeared "all of them were from the fighting in Bint Jbail," a border village that was the scene of some of the heaviest ground fighting in the battle between Israel and Hezbollah, Mroue said.

One fighter was brought in with serious stomach wounds, and part of his intestine was removed in an operation, but the same day he was taken from the hospital and transferred to medical facilities outside Tyre, Mroue said.

Mroue knows the danger of keeping Hezbollah fighters in his hospital.

In the 1982 Israeli invasion of Lebanon, when Jabal Amal was brand new, Israeli warplanes bombed the hospital, wiping out the top two floors - though the facility had also treated some Israeli soldiers, Mroue said.

Israeli commandos then stormed the hospital and snatched three Hezbollah fighters being treated.

At the outset of the current hostilities in July, Mroue said he contacted the International Committee of the Red Cross and the United Nations to tell them - and pass onto the Israelis - that Jabal Amal would not admit Hezbollah fighters to stay.

"If we try to maintain soldiers of Hezbollah we will take a big risk," Mroue said.

Lebanon's Health Ministry and security officials say their counts of the dead don't include Hezbollah fighters. Their counts are based on bodies at hospitals or retrieved by rescue workers at the scene. The situation at Jabal Amal and other hospitals in the south suggests that very few guerrillas have gotten mixed up in the count, since few come through the facilities.

Nearly 80 percent of casualties in the south - where the bulk of bombardment and fighting has taken place - have come through Jabal Amal hospital, and Mroue said the facility had had only the one dead guerrila.

In Nabatiyeh, another main city in the south, nine wounded guerrillas have come through the main Ghandour Hospital, and no Hezbollah deaths reported, said Dr. Aref Choueib. Like Jabal Amal, the wounded guerrillas were quickly shipped elsewhere after emergency treatment.

Hezbollah has acknowledged that 65 fighters have been killed in the conflict - but Israel says the guerrillas' toll is far higher, at least 200. Hezbollah generally takes care of its own dead and wounded, sending only the worst of the wounded for treatment at hospitals, so there is no way to independently confirm the toll.

The Health Ministry and security officials have reported 667 civilians killed, and 31 Lebanese soldiers have also died, according to the Lebanese military.

Meanwhile, hospitals in Tyre - the biggest city south of the Litani River - have struggled with the influx of casualties.

Since the outbreak of war, Tyre has held two mass funerals, burying more than 100 dead. A third mass burial was to be held last week but was delayed after Israeli attacks on and near the city intensified. The dead have been buried in shallow graves in numbered coffins to be exhumed later by family members for burial at home.

On Thursday, all patients were evacuated from Tyre's 80-bed Hiram Hospital, because doctors feared a new wave of wounded as Israel launched a new assault ahead of a prospective cease-fire, said Dr. Haider Jouni.

Israel has warned that any vehicle on the roads of south Lebanon will be destroyed on suspicion of carrying Hezbollah supplies, and for the past week it has not given safe passage for aid convoys to the south.

Mroue said Jabal Amal is running out of basic food supplies for patients, staff and refugees because of the stoppage of aid. Israel allowed a relief ship carrying food to dock in Tyre on Saturday, and the fuel that arrived would be shared with the hospitals in Tyre, said Red Cross spokesman Roland Huguenin.

Ambulances in the city, meanwhile, are risking movement on the roads - they have beeen ferrying some 70 dialysis patients and other chemotherapy patients to and from their homes to Jabal Ali for treatment.
