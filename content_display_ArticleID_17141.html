Creative Bedtime Rituals: Ending the Day with a Jewish Connection

By Debra Nussbaum Cohen


When I was pregnant with my son, Aryeh, who is now 17 months old, I promised myself I'd do something Jewish him before tucking him into bed at night.

The problem was, I didn't know where to begin. I didn't have a Jewish bedtime ritual growing up and so I lacked any personal experience on which to draw. I looked in a couple of prayer books but didn't feel strongly connected to the traditional bedtime reading of the Sh'ma , the prayer declaring God's oneness].

Though the paragraphs surrounding the central line of the Sh'ma are beautiful, they're very formal and seemed too intricate, too heavy, to recite with my baby boy.

My religious experience since Ari's birth has been richer than anything that ever preceded his arrival. But it's also been informal and spontaneous, which I prefer over trying to pray in temple on Shabbat while feeding an infant or keeping an active toddler out of trouble.

Instead, the most wonderful moments of connection with the divine energy that fuels the universe have been as Ari lies relaxed in my arms, gazing up into my eyes while sucking lazily on his first bottle of the morning or his last one at night. It's the quiet times, the lulls between the structure and activity of our days, that are the most precious and full of prayer.

I wanted to find a bedtime ritual that matches these moments, a way that feels just as comfortable to teach Ari to feel connected to, and protected by, the divine power of the universe.

Something that my downstairs neighbor, an evangelical Christian, recently said reminded me that I needed to begin developing our own ritual. We were chatting one evening when she said she had to go say prayers with her son and daughter.

My neighbor, whose faith in God is steadfast and a constant thread woven throughout her family's life, was born a Jew.

When she began wondering about God and faith, she was approached by evangelical Christians, who invited her into a life of connection with God. When she turned to a rabbi she knew, challenging him for Jewish answers to her questions, he was unable to provide her with meaning. So she turned to those who offered her the promise of a relationship with spirituality.

Her experience has been a powerful reminder to me that if I don't give Aryeh a sense of spiritual intimacy with God, then someone else might.

What follows is an unscientific sampling of creative Jewish bedtime (and some other) rituals developed by friends who are striving to find their own way to bring their children closer to that elusive, longed for connection with God.

Cara Saposnik of Brooklyn, who spent most of her youth living in Israel, and her husband, Rich Kirschen, rock their 15-month-old daughter Gili to sleep singing two Israeli songs--one by Natan Alterman about the Jezreel Valley going to sleep, and the other, the pop song "Yehiyeh Tov," which means "It Will Be Good."

For the second six months of Gili's life, Kirshen, who is a Reform rabbi, would get up with her at dawn and take her to the dining room window, where they would watch night turn into day.

"She watched it with such intensity, so amazed as she looked at everything," Kirschen says. "This impromptu ritual stopped my jadedness and opened me up to each new day. We were very present and didn't have to fill the space with words.

Daniel Lehmann, an Orthodox rabbi and the principal of a Baltimore day school, says the traditional Sh'ma with his son Hillel, who is three.

Several months ago, after they read the Sh'ma out loud in Hillel's Jewish nursery school class, the boy began his own new supplement. As Hillel says, "Adonai is One," he extends his pointer finger, which Lehmann touches with his own extended finger, like Adam and God do in Michelangelo's fresco of the moment of creation.

Shai Gluskin, a Reconstructionist rabbi in Philadelphia, sings two Hebrew songs, which he learned as a boy in, to his daughter, Sophia Peral, as she goes to sleep--an Israeli song called "Lilah, Lilah," and an Israeli song sung to the tune of "Taps." The English translation of the lyrics is, "It's the end of the day/The sun is silent/Stars are glimmering In the sky/Night has come for us to rest/Peace, wholeness, peace."

In the early morning, just after Sophia has had her first drink of the day and she is lying in bed with her parents, "all smiley and perky," says Gluskin, he says the first line of the Modeh Ani with her. This is the morning prayer of gratitude to God for returning one's soul, and it is traditionally said upon waking.

Larry Magarik, a lawyer in New York City, began making bedtime music and prayer tapes for his sons when his oldest, Benjamin, was three.

The first one that Magarik compiled was about welcoming Shabbat with Kiddush and some zemiort, Sabbath songs, and niggunim, wordless tunes.

Then Magarik compiled a Goodnight to Ben tape, on which he recites the Sh'ma. Shlomo Carlebach also sang, "Behold God Neither Slumbers Nor Sleeps," and a story about how God is watching over little Jewish children.

Ben, at 11, doesn't usually listen to his father's tapes anymore, but his younger brothers, Raphael, 5, and Yasha, 3, share a room and often listen to them as they go to sleep.

Melanie Schneider, director of a Reconstructionist organization in New York, puts her 16-month-old daughter, Sasha, to sleep with the "Cha Sh'ma." Schneider recites the Ve-ahavta paragraph following the Sh'ma, which has a lot of words ending with the sound cha. At the appropriate moments at the ends of the words, Schneider pauses and points to Sasha, who understands her cue and says, with great enthusiasm and many giggles, "Cha!"

When Susan Grossman's six-year-old son Yonaton was younger and afraid of the dark, she developed a prayer about the angels on each side of him, protecting him.

When Yonaton gets all comfy in bed, she sings him the prayer about the angel Michael on his right, Gabriel on his left, Uriel at his feet, Raphael at the had of the bed, and above him, like a canopy, watching over Israel and him as he sleeps, is Shechinat-El, or God's Presence. Then they say the central line of the Sh'ma.

In traditional "angelology," says Grossman, a Conservative rabbi in Tuckahoe, New York, the angel Micahel's flaming sword scares away frightening monsters; Gabriel's horn calls the celestial beings to his aid; Uriel lights a path with a flaming torch; Raphael is a healer; and Shechinat-El protects children from harm.

When her daughter, Frances was three, Nancy Fuchs-Kreimer, billowed the girl's top bed sheet in the air, letting it fall down softly on her while they sang a Hebrew song, "Spread Over Us Your Tabernacle of Peace," which is taken from a line in the traditional evening prayers.

Frances also has on her wall the Sh'ma and a poem: "The world is full of wonder/I see it every day/And so before I close my eyes/I thank God and I pray."

They told each other of the wonders of the day, little or big things, says Fuchs-Kreimer, a Reconstructionst rabbi in Philadelphia who is writing a book about the spiritual journey of parenting. Then they recited the Sh'ma.

The first time Anna Sasson, 8, rode her bike without training wheels, she fell off. As she got up, her father, Tanny Sasson, taught her to say, Adonai li velo irah, or "God is with me and I will not fear."

Now, says her mother, Helen Zelon, a nonfiction writer in Brooklyn, Anna repeats it to comfort herself when she has frightful thoughts. Sometimes as Anna is falling off to sleep at night, Zelon hears her whisper, Adonai li velo irah.

The Sh'ma

Shema Israel Adonia Elohenu Adonai Echad. Hear, O Israel, Adonai is our God, Adonai is One. Blessed be the name of God's glorious majesty forever and ever.

You shall love the Adonai your God with all your heart, and with all your soul, and with all your might. And these words which I command you today shall be in your heart. You shall teach them diligently to your children, and you shall speak of them when you are sitting at home and when you go on a journey, when you lie down and when you rise up. You shall bind them for a sign on your hand, and they shall be for frontlets between your eyes. You shall inscribe them on the door posts of your house and on your gates.

Mute, invisible, God lived in my house when I was little, along with my parents and my brother. God was in my room when I woke up in the morning, had a place at our table during meal times, and watched over me while I slept.

I loved the mystery of this Force that felt close enough to hold the whole world in His or Her palm. So it was logical that the language of this God was, to my English ear, a stream of foreign sounds.

I memorized many of them by the time I was four. At night my parents turned out the lights and together we would recite the Sh'ma. I never asked them what the words meant nor did I need to know.

I saw it, almost, as a secret code letting God know it was time for guard duty. Then, while I drifted into sleep, my parents read me a bedtime story.

I've known Hebrew fluently for many years. But each night before I go to sleep, I recite this ancient prayer, slipping for a moment away from comprehension and returning to that moment on the edge of knowing, when mystery rocked me to sleep.


Debra Nussbaum Cohen is a staff writer for The New York Jewish Week, and the mother of 2 children.
