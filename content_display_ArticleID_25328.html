A Ground Zero Hanukkah

By Jonathan Mark


A menorah is lit on the first night of Hanukkah near the remains of the World Trade Center complex.
In wartime, Hanukkah ceases to be the most infantile of Jewish holidays and is restored to its essence of dignity and defiance. These are the eight days of death and dust, blood and broken glass, flames on candles and fire in the streets.

At Ground Zero, Col. Jacob Goldstein -- a Chabad rabbi sent to the National Guard by the Lubavitcher rebbe nearly 30 years ago, and now the New York Guard's ranking chaplain -- walks through the rubble and the sickly perfume of dead bodies and fire, trying to find a place for a Ground Zero menorah. The dust of the dead will coat the menorah like it coats the soldiers. Goldstein and his men will plant that menorah like it was the flag at Iwo Jima.

A Hanukkah story: They thought the Ground Zero fire would only last one day but it has lasted until Hanukkah. The smoke still curls over Goldstein's shoulder.

Less than six blocks away, at the Museum of Jewish Heritage - A Living Memorial to the Holocaust, Yossi S., a yeshiva student from Belle Harbor looks at other menorahs that were once coated with dust. There, behind glass: A menorah hammered out by tinsmiths when that was a capital crime. There, behind glass: A menorah that a Czech woman found in her own rubble after returning to her home that wasn't her home anymore.

Now Yossi's Belle Harbor has a Ground Zero all its own. Yossi can't really say what's brought him to Ground Zero and the museum of Jewish Ground Zero. He'd never thought to come here before that plane exploded into his neighborhood. "There's a God," he says quietly.

A few feet away, a non-Jewish student taking "Holocaust lit" at Nassau Community College is taking notes on other artifacts behind glass: An ArtScroll siddur; a Soviet Jewry button from the 1970s; a Flatbush yearbook from 1953; a Camp Ramah songbook from 1967. She watches a video of poet-singer Leonard Cohen and a clip of an Afghanistan synagogue. She takes notes about a poster from Leonard Bernstein's "Journey to Jerusalem," filmed three weeks after the Six-Day War: "The tears...the joy ... the Wailing Wall... a kibbutz ... Commemorate the reunification of Jerusalem." There is life, says the museum, after the end of the world.

What would you save if the world was coming to an end? What would you write about? Does anyone remember the way August looked in 1939 or 1969 or 2001? A museum exhibit (through Feb. 18) tells the story of wartime Warsaw Saturdays when the friends of one Emanuel Ringelblum, calling themselves "Oneg Shabbos" -- with equal parts sarcasm and sentimentality -- started collecting an archive of their ghetto lives. It was essentially a Jewish newspaper delivered not to your mailbox but to tin boxes and metal milk cans that they buried in the rubble for ... who? Would there still be Jews in the world? Who would find it? "Oneg Shabbos" collected everything, the ephemeral, the ethereal: Hebrew candy wrappers and chronicles of hunger; children's poems and dead men's paintings; ghetto theater programs and detailed maps of Treblinka.

At Ground Zero, an Oneg Shabbos all its own. In the fluttering papers -- a yeshiva's tuition bill; a letter from a summer camp. Someone found a yarmulke and gave it to Rabbi Goldstein.

"When he handed it to me," says Goldstein, "I opened it and burst into tears." It was a yarmulke inscribed from a wedding, Sept. 9.

The first Shabbat post 9-11, an Oneg Shabbos for Goldstein, pawing through the dirt, looking for the living.

From his skyscraper window, David Marwell, the museum's director, tries to point to where the World Trade Center was, a few blocks away in the haze. It was through these windows that his staff watched the towers burn. Suddenly, it seems worth knowing that this Holocaust museum was built on earth excavated during the construction of the World Trade Center -- Ground Zeroes united.

"We were right in the middle of preparing the Ringelblum exhibition when Sept. 11 happened," says Marwell. "I don't think anyone failed to remark that on some level we were doing what they were doing under incredibly more sinister circumstances: They had this notion, a decided, calculated attempt to collect material expressing their lives and world."

At Ground Zero, Rabbi Goldstein walks near a wall where mourners bring mementos to the rubble's edge; a favorite beer can, a football souvenir, a photograph.

"Heartbreak Ridge," says Goldstein. He looks at a poster of the dead, looking for his own: "Where's Mack?" he says to himself. There, a photo of Walter McNeil, first sergeant of the 369th, "the Harlem Hell Fighters," says the chasid. "We went to the Gulf War together. I didn't have a cot to sleep on when I pulled into Fort Dix. He found me a bed."

He says, "Dominguez?" as if someone could hear. He finds the photo: Jerome Dominguez of Emergency Services. "He was with the 105th Air Mobility Wing, a reservist," says the chasid. "Another one of our guys."

Are bodies still being found on the eve of Hanukkah? "We're finding some parts ..."

In what would be for most soldiers an ammo pouch, Goldstein carries Tehillim, the Book of Psalms. He reads the daily portion, for his soldiers and Israel's. When suicide bombers exploded through Jerusalem and Haifa, "it reminded me of Ground Zero," says Goldstein. "This morning when I opened up the paper, I was crying. The body parts, the pools of blood, the evil. The first days here flashed right back in my face."

He has to stop to wipe tears from his eyes. He talks about Al Ha'Nisim, Hanukkah's singular prayer about the "evil kingdom" that started a war against "the pure."

He carries three cards in his ammo pouch, guidelines for a dying soldier, according to Catholic, Protestant or Jewish rites. The army suggests that these prayers be carried "on the person of all company commanders and all platoon leaders at all times."

Young men will die before winter's end. Says the army card: "If a dying soldier (Jewish) desires religious ministration, and there is no chaplain within reach, his commanding officer or platoon leader should repeat with him, one, the Shema; two, the [Viduy] confession for the critically ill; and three, the 23rd Psalm." The card contains English translations.

The rabbi-colonel also carries a plastic card issued by the Israeli army, a prayer to be said before combat: "Shema Yisrael, when you're preparing to go to war against your enemies ... Don't be afraid. God will be with you in combat and bring you victory."

At last, the rabbi found a place for the Ground Zero menorah. "We'll put it over there, where they are picking away at those heaps."

On the nights of Hanukkah, Jews will sing Maoz Tzur at Ground Zero: "They breached the walls of my towers. ... Avenge Your servant's blood from the evil nation."

"Whoever comes down here," says Col. Goldstein, "will know that there's a Hanukkah in the world."
