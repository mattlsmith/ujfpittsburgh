Facts and Figures



Fact #1
Federation dollars help support Jewish day schools, which have grown into the most popular form of Jewish education for American Jewish children. According to findings from the National Jewish Population Survey (NJPS) 2000-01, while only 12% of adults age 18-34 attended day school, the current proportion among children age 6-17 has jumped to 29%. Additional findings also reveal stronger levels of Jewish identity among adults who have attended day school than among those who did not.

Fact #2
With the help of a $975,000 grant from the International Commission on Holocaust Era Insurance Claims (ICHEIC), the Jewish Agency launched a three-week day camp to increase basic Jewish cultural literacy for young Jews in the former Soviet Union. To date, 700 young people have participated in the project, which includes Holocaust studies.

Fact #3 
Federation-supported Jewish renewal programs in the former Soviet Union have helped lay the foundation for a Jewish renaissance. To date, 182 Jewish Community Centers, 184 Jewish libraries, 60 Jewish kindergartens and 27 Jewish day schools have been established throughout the FSU, where an estimated 1.3 million* Jews still live. * (estimates differ widely)...

Fact #4
The federation-sponsored Children's Initiative has provided relief to more than 14,000 impoverished children in the former Soviet Union since the project's launch in 2002. Working with local communities, the Children's Initiative provides support for existing services for children such as emergency one-time cash assistance for clothing, medicine, food, vitamins and therapy.

Fact #5
In the summer of 2004, 7,000 children ranging from pre-school age to 17 attended federation-sponsored camps in dozens of locations throughout the former Soviet Union. Week-long camp sessions combined recreational activities with educational and cultural programs designed to provide a sense of Jewish identity. For many of the youngsters, this was their first exposure to Judaism.

Fact #6
Of the 1.5 million school-age children in Israel, 600,000 live below the poverty line and cannot afford the cost of basic school supplies. Twenty percent of these children are olim. Last year alone, 4,400 new immigrant children dropped out of school. To combat this recent trend, Making the Grade, a federation-supported initiative, provides 20,000 at-risk children in grades 1-6 with necessary equipment including school bags, text books, binders, pencils and calculators.

Fact #7
Hundreds of thousands of Jewish schoolchildren throughout the world have returned to their classrooms. To strengthen the centrality of Israel and Zionism in the curriculum, federation-supported programs send hundreds of Israeli educators to teach in more than 40 countries and regions including North America, Europe, the former Soviet Union, Latin America, as well as Asia, Africa, and Oceania.

Fact #8
Since the beginning of 2004, more than 15,000 people have enrolled and studied at 283 federation-sponsored ulpanim throughout the former Soviet Union. The ulpanim network provides Hebrew-language courses, which help potential olim successfully integrate into Israeli society and provide an introduction to Zionism and Israel.

Fact #9
Close to 450 Americans and Canadians, ranging from two weeks of age to 71 years old, arrived at Ben Gurion Airport in Tel-Aviv mid-July as new immigrants -- the largest group of North Americans ever to immigrate to Israel on a single flight. By the end of August some 1,000 North Americans had made aliyah, organized by the U.S.-based Nefesh B'Nefesh, in cooperation with the Jewish Agency and the endorsement of federations.

Fact #10
Federation dollars are helping the remaining Jews of Iraq. A once-thriving community of one quarter million, today only a maximum of 35 people remain, largely elderly people who lived clandestinely under Saddam Hussein and survived the latest war. The American Jewish Joint Distribution Committee has provided food and medicine to these last Jews, though more support is needed to help them and less than a dozen others who have made aliyah with the help of the Jewish Agency and HIAS.

Fact #11
With an estimated one million Jews in the former Soviet Union, the Jewish Agency has expanded its activities in the region. In the first six months of 2004, 57 emissaries served in 22 Jewish Agency posts in the FSU, sponsoring activities in 227 locations. During that time, some 80,000 people took part in federation-supported programs throughout the FSU, and 4,000 people immigrated to Israel.

Fact #12

For Israeli high school dropouts facing a lifetime of broken dreams, the federation-supported "It's a Deal" program offers a brighter future by building entrepreneurial skills. Since 1998, businesses have functioned as investors and mentors for 600 at-risk youth who have launched and managed new ventures such as an employee café at a high-tech firm, a landscaping outfit and a petting zoo for children.

Fact #13
Hefziba, a federation-supported educational network in the former Soviet Union, allows over 10,000 youngsters in grades 1-11 to attend more than 43 Jewish day schools and 100 Sunday schools. The Hefziba network also develops educational materials and provides training for Jewish teachers in a teachers college setting. Ongoing support is vital for the continuity of Jewish education in the FSU.

Fact #14
An estimated one in four U.S. households are involved with caring for elderly or disabled family members. With the National Jewish Population Survey finding that 19 percent of American Jews are aged 65 or older -- up two percentage points since 1990 -- an increasing number of American Jews are experiencing the emotional and financial challenges of care-giving. The federation system provides caregivers information and referrals, support groups and respite care, all of which require continued support to meet this growing need.

Fact #15
Once a cornerstone of Israeli society, the kibbutz faces increasing decline as the economy grows more industrialized. Second Chance, a federation-supported initiative, has awarded 800 former kibbutz and moshav members scholarships and other aid to attend college or university. As demand for Second Chance grows, the program needs support to help meet the shifting needs of Israelis.

Fact #16
Israeli Olympic athletes who have immigrated from Ethiopia and the former Soviet Union are receiving $1,500 monthly thanks to a Jewish Agency and federation grant. The stipends, which run for nine months, are worth more than the average Israeli salary. The funds will allow 15 Olympians from Ethiopia and two from the FSU to focus on training rather than on the challenges of finding work.

Fact #17
More than 80,000 Israeli preschoolers living in areas most vulnerable to terrorist attacks will be protected by security guards this coming school year. Guards will watch over 1,116 kindergartens in 116 communities, covering 2,861 classes. Kindergarten security is funded with $3 million, as part of the latest $10 million Israel Emergency Campaign allocations.

Fact #18
The American Jewish population is aging faster than the general population. More than a million Jews are over 65; more than 318,000 live alone. Federation-supported Friendly Visitor programs, transportation assistance, home-delivered meals and a myriad of other ground-breaking services help ensure that our seniors are cared for with dignity and loving kindness. As state and federal budget cuts threaten our base of services, the need to support seniors in their homes is increasing daily.

Fact #19
Some 30,000 disadvantaged Israeli students attend academic and professional training programs thanks to federation-supported initiatives. Over the past 30 years, JAFI, in conjunction with the Israeli government, has built 10 colleges and supported academic and professional training. With the help of federation funds, scholarships are provided to thousands of new immigrants and veteran Israelis to pursue a higher education. With continued efforts we can help bridge educational and socio-economic gaps in Israel and give participants a chance to succeed.

Fact #20
There is a direct correlation between exposure to trauma and violence and development of Post Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD). Not surprisingly, more than 220,000 Israeli children suffer from clinical PTSD and while the violence has decreased, the number is on the rise; immigrant children and youth living in high-risk areas are particularly vulnerable. Federation-supported programs are working to help these children with counseling and school-based programs, but not everyone is being reached. Efforts must be expanded and continued to help families and communities face the reality of the matzav.

Fact #21
22.1% of Americans 18 and older suffer from a diagnosable mental disorder.* Federations and their affiliated agencies provide mental health services to some 500,000 adults and 370,000 children across the nation including counseling, foster care, school-based programs, vocational skills training and group homes. Your ongoing support is necessary to ensure that we expand the scope of services and continue to advocate for this population.

* National Institute of Mental Health

Fact #22
Teenage olim are dropping out of high school at a significantly higher rate than other Israeli teens. Gamla -- a federation-supported program -- mobilizes 1,300 volunteers, many of whom are retired teachers, to provide intensive, one-on-one tutoring in 33 Israeli cities to 1,450 students from Ethiopian, Kavkazi, Bucharan and other immigrant communities. Programs like Gamla not only help potential dropouts but also foster a sense of social responsibility among Israelis.

Fact #23
Today in Argentina, more than 900 Jewish babies under the age of three live in poverty and face the possibility of undernourishment. A new federation-supported initiative -- Baby Help -- provides medical and food assistance to babies, children and at-risk pregnant women. Additional funds are needed to provide similar services to approximately 1,000 impoverished older children, ages 3-6.

Fact #24
Selah* was established in 1995 to encourage aliyah by providing Jewish youth from the former Soviet Union with financial and educational opportunities they would not get in their countries of origin. Since the program's inception, over 7,000 college-age students have made aliyah and 40% of the students' parents have followed in their footsteps. Selah, a one-year program, includes an intensive ulpan as well as academic preparatory programs at Israeli universities. Initiatives like these need our continued support to help integrate bright young olim into Israeli society.

* (Selah is a Hebrew acronym for "Students Before Parents")

Fact #25
Did you know? Federation dollars help support JDC's Hesed welfare centers in the former Soviet Union. In 2003 the Hesed network: distributed 1,352,221 food packages; provided 2,827,259 meals in communal dining rooms; delivered 2,984,676 meals-on-wheels to 13,148 clients; organized 762 "Warm Homes" for 10,412 elderly; made home-care visits to 26,086 homebound people; brought winter relief to more than 68,000 people; and provided prescription medications to 110,000 elderly. More funds are necessary to expand the scope of these vital services to a poor and aging community.

Fact #26
There are thousands of new immigrant soldiers in the Israeli army, 2,500 of whom have no immediate relatives in Israel. They often join the army at a considerable disadvantage lacking financial resources, Hebrew language skills and family support. The Jewish Agency for Israel's Fund for Immigrant Soldiers Alone in Israel is designed to offer a range of services, from housing and basic living expenses to pre-military prep courses. These brave olim need our continued support to ensure successful completion of their army service.

Fact #27
Through federation funds, JDC has recently launched AMEN -- a Hebrew acronym for Youth Volunteer City. The program is mobilizing some 4,000 youth and young adults in eight locations throughout Israel to take a leading role in local volunteer activities. By strengthening volunteer resources, AMEN not only aims to help Israeli society deal with its growing social needs, but also strives to deepen the foundations of civic responsibility.

Fact #28
Did you know? The federation system takes seniors to doctors, grocery stores, kosher meal sites and Jewish cultural activities approximately 1,000 times a day. Additional funds would help expand these services for the nearly one million elderly Jews in the United States, many of whom depend on our senior transportation programs as their sole link to the community.

Fact #29

In the United States, approximately 350,000 children and adults in Jewish households live below the poverty line and close to 500,000 Jews live in low-income homes. Federation dollars help provide emergency cash assistance, food programs, low-income housing and job placement assistance. More money is needed to expand home health care programs, affordable child-care services and housing options for this population.

Fact #30

70 years ago Henrietta Szold and Recha Freier founded Youth Aliyah, which has since helped 300,000 Israeli youngsters reclaim their lives and futures. Today, the Jewish Agency, through the assistance of federation, Hadassah and a worldwide network of supporters, operates five Youth Aliyah villages. Every year, the program rescues hundreds of Israel's most disadvantaged from the cycle of poverty, giving them a second chance to become a productive part of Israeli society.

Fact #31
Of the nearly 122,000 Holocaust survivors in the United States, one in four is living below the poverty line. The federation system currently provides an array of services including in-home care, transportation, case management and emergency financial assistance. To meet the growing needs of this aging and extremely frail population, more funds would ensure that these vital programs are expanded.

Fact #32
Did you know? Every year 1.7 million Americans take part in a federation-supported counseling program. For example: 8,000 participate in programs for victims of abuse; 100,000 in drug counseling programs; and 15,000 in programs for the developmentally disabled.

Fact #33
In the former Soviet Union, JDC provides food packages, meals-on-wheels, homecare, winter relief and medicine to 240,000 elderly Jews -- half of them rely on federation dollars for their subsistence. Due to limited funds, our services have been significantly cut back. In some cases, financial support has been reduced from $20 a month to $10, which means that people once eligible for five soup kitchen meals a week, now receive just one food package every month. More money is needed to help these elderly Jews live out their lives in dignity.

Fact #34
There's a strong relationship between travel to Israel and a positive Jewish identity.  For adults who traveled to Israel between the ages of 14-26, 67% say being Jewish is "very important" compared with 29% who did not travel to Israel in their youth. Results are similar for adults who attended Jewish summer camps or participated in Jewish youth groups.* Federation support of informal Jewish education for teens and young adults is critical for the continuity of the North American Jewish community. (*NJPS 2000-2001)

Fact #35
One in ten Americans over the age of 65 has Alzheimer's and half of those over 85 have symptoms of Alzheimer's. With nearly 1,000,000 American Jews 65 and older, and as that population continues to grow, our communities are faced with the challenge of providing for the needs of Alzheimer's patients and their family caregivers. Federation dollars currently fund information and referral programs, case management services and in-home support. But more money is needed to expand adult day care programs, provide respite care to caregivers and offer support and counseling to those affected.

Fact #36
In Israel, 16.1% of men and 20.2% of women in the Ethiopian community are unemployed. This is approximately twice the rate of the general Jewish population -- 8.3% of men and 9.1% of women. The Jewish Agency, JDC and the Ethiopian National Project (ENP) currently provide a variety of federation-supported intervention programs to increase employment, including training for discharged soldiers, employment incentives for teachers, and work placement services. But it's not enough. More funds are needed to expand services in the areas of vocational training, identifying job opportunities and employment. As more Ethiopians join the workforce, the entire community will enjoy greater participation in Israeli society.

Fact #37
Today in Argentina, 36,391 Jews receive welfare relief at 75 federation-supported Social Assistance Centers. This compares with 39 centers, assisting 16,627 Jews in 2002. Even with this increase, we are still offering support and relief to only half the number of Jews who are in need -- an estimated 65,000 impoverished in a population of 200,000 Jews. More money is urgently needed for basics like food and medicine. (JDC, 2004)

Fact #38
31% of all 17-year-olds in the Caucasus (Kavkazi) community in Israel dropped out of school in 1997. By 2003, this number was reduced to 18%, according to a Brookdale Institute study. The drop was due in great part to federation-supported education, after-school and intervention programs. Though the improvement is impressive, it's not enough in a country where the overall dropout rate is 10%. With our continued support, more Kavkazi youth will be able to finish high school. 
References:

1. National study of Caucasus youth, 1997 & 2002, JDC-Brookdale Institute

2. Statistical abstract of the national council for the child in Israel, 2002

Fact #39
Every year federation-supported programs deliver 1,025,000 meals through the meals-on-wheels program, 260,000 meals at communal sites, and another 150,000 meals through food banks. In total, more than 1.4 million meals are served every year. There are still many more who are hungry. Additional funds would help feed those in need.

Fact #40
In 2003, a reported 1,989 Israeli children were admitted into hospital emergency rooms with injuries caused by family violence. Almost half were children under five. Thanks to federation-supported programs we are able to provide services to victims and their families, including school and community based intervention, family counseling, and afternoon enrichment programs. With more dollars, we could help many more children.
