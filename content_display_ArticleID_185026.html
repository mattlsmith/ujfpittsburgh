Israel's Olmert visits Europe seeking support for West Bank withdrawal plan

By STEVE WEIZMAN (Associated Press Writer)
Associated Press
06/11/2006


LONDON - Israeli Prime Minister Ehud Olmert arrived in Britain Sunday at the start of a European tour aimed at bolstering international support for his contentious plan to unilaterally redraw Israel's borders with the Palestinians.

Olmert, who was sworn in as premier last month, was to meet British Prime Minister Tony Blair in London on Monday and then fly to Paris for talks with French President Jacques Chirac and others.

"I am going to present my plan as I have in my other foreign trips," he told reporters as he boarded his London-bound flight in Israel.

Olmert has visited Washington, Egypt and Jordan in a campaign to win backing for the plan.

He said in an interview aired on Sky Television Sunday that he was "ready to make concessions that will be painful and divisive."

Olmert and the European leaders are also expected to discuss their worries over Iran's nuclear program.

Europe and America are seeking to persuade Iran to suspend its uranium enrichment program, which the West suspects is aimed at producing a nuclear bomb but Tehran says is meant for nuclear power plants.

Iranian President Mahmoud Ahmadinejad fueled fears about his country's intentions when he said Israel should be wiped off the map.

"Israel will not tolerate the possession of nuclear weapons in the hands of those who say that they want to wipe Israel off the map," Olmert told Sky. "I hope that America and Europe will deal with it effectively."

Olmert wants international support for his plan to withdraw settlers and soldiers from large parts of the West Bank and set Israel's final border with the territory.

Europe, the United States and moderate Arab states have pressed him to try negotiations with Palestinian leader Mahmoud Abbas before taking unilateral steps. But prospects for formal peace talks between the two sides have weakened dramatically since the militant Hamas took control of the Palestinian government in January elections.

Olmert has said he will give the Palestinians until the end of the year to negotiate a peace deal. If this fails, he says, Israel will evacuate most but not all of the West Bank, removing tens of thousands of Jewish settlers, on its own.

The Palestinians object to what they would view as a seizure of key parts of their land, while critics in Israel argue that the plan gives too much in exchange for no peace commitment from the Palestinians.

Israel withdrew unilaterally from all of the Gaza Strip last year.

Olmert told Sky that he would seek Blair's help to "facilitate this process that will hopefully force the Palestinians to change their minds so that they will be ready for the negotiations as well."

Olmert has said he will meet with Abbas to explore the possibility of peace talks, but Israeli officials say it will be difficult to hold negotiations as long as Hamas remains committed to Israel's destruction.

"We want to negotiate with (Abbas), we will be meeting with him and I plan to see him later this month and see what I can do to help him," Olmert told Sky.

"But if it will be agreed by the international community that conditions have not matured to allow negotiations, which is the case now, then the question is what shall we do. Wait forever? Do nothing? Keep a stalemate, freeze the situation? Or shall we try to do something that will move things forward that will help create an environment of better understanding? That's what I'm interested in doing and I don't think that we should wait too much."
