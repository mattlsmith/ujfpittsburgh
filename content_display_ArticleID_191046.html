August 7, 2006/ 13 Av 5766

Ekev

“…and so that you will prolong your days on the Land that Hashem swore to your forefathers to give them and to their offspring---a land flowing with milk and honey…” 

In this week’s Torah portion the above verse stands out as a reminder of what lies in store for the People of Israel. In the Talmud (Ketubot 111b) there is a lovely story which describes Rabbi Rami bar Yechezkel visiting Bnei Brak when he sees some goats eating beneath a fig tree. Honey began to ooze from the ripe figs, milk dripped from the goats, and the two combined into one flowing stream.

This is the meaning, he exclaimed, of "a land flowing with milk and honey!"

This is the story of the graphic description of the extraordinary bounty overflowing with wholesome and tasty natural resources. Perhaps the significance of his discovery is that not only is Eretz Yisrael blessed with wholesome and tasty natural resources, but that these two seemingly disparate dimensions of food are naturally and perfectly blended for the health and enjoyment of the inhabitants of the land "flowing with milk and honey."

But let us not think that the virtues of the land are automatic. It obviously takes a great deal of hard work to produce the bounty from the land. During my sabbatical a few years ago I witnessed first-hand how extraordinary is the blessing of the land which G-d gave to our Jewish People. I had the opportunity to work the land when I was invited by Momi, my brother-in-law, to participate in the harvest of nine fields of bell peppers which he and my sister co-own on Moshav Faran, just an hour’s drive from Eilat in the Arava Desert. I listened to stories of how Momi and his partner Yossi founded the Moshav in 1972 when they lived in tents while they prepared the desert soil to become host for the seeds that would blossom into some of the most delectable vegetables in the world. Now the Moshav boasts one of the most successful agricultural enterprises in all of Israel, exporting vegetables, flowers and other products to Europe, the U.S. and other countries throughout the world.

The lesson for all of us is to recognize, like those who are blessed to live in Israel, that we must not take anything for granted. When we go to the grocery store, do we ever think about the amount of time and effort went into producing those fruits and vegetables we purchase? Never in human experience has food been available in the staggering abundance seen in North America today. We are awash in edibles shipped in from around the world; seasonality has largely disappeared. Food obtrudes itself constantly, seductively, into our lives—on sidewalks, in airplanes, at gas stations and movie theaters. Let us remember that G-d blessed us with the ability to make use of land in order to provide us the food which we need to exist. But let us also remember that it is only through hard work and dedication that our lives will be truly enriched with the bounty for which we are provided.

Rabbi Reuven Taff, a member of the UJC Rabbinic Cabinet is rabbi of Mosaic Law Congregation in Sacramento, California. He can be reached at rabbi@mosaiclaw.org.
